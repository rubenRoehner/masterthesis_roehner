\section{Hyperparameter Optimization}\label{sec:evaluation-ho}
The performance of reinforcement learning agents is highly dependent on the selection of their hyperparameters.
A systematic hyperparameter optimization (HO) is therefore essential to ensure agent efficacy, particularly for a complex hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework such as the one proposed.
A dedicated HO process was conducted for both the Regional Distribution Coordinator (RDC) and the User-Incentive Coordinator (UIC) agents to maximize their individual performance.
This section outlines the optimization methodology and presents the final hyperparameter configurations used in the subsequent evaluation of the HRL framework

The optimization objective for both agents was to maximize a composite reward function.
This function is a weighted sum of three metrics: maximizing satisfied user demand ($R_{demand}$), minimizing rebalancing costs ($R_{cost}$), and promoting equitable vehicle distribution ($R_{gini}$).\\

For the HO process, the Optuna framework was utilized, a state-of-the-art optimization tool \cite{akiba_optuna_2019}.
The Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) was selected as the search algorithm due to its effectiveness in complex, high-dimensional search spaces \cite{akiba_optuna_2019}.
TPE probabilistically models the search space to efficiently identify promising hyperparameter configurations.
The algorithm fits a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) to a set of well-performing parameter configurations and calculates a ratio against a GMM fitted to the remaining configurations.
The sampler then selects the parameter value that maximizes this ratio, guiding the search toward more promising regions \cite{akiba_optuna_2019}.

To ensure reproducibility, all optimization runs were seeded.
As HO is computationally intensive, the process was parallelized across multiple jobs.
Furthermore, demand forecasts were pre-calculated to reduce computation time. The remainder of this section details the specific HO processes for the RDC and UIC agents.

\subsection{Regional Distribution Coordinator (RDC)}\label{sec:evaluation-ho-rdc}
The RDC agent, based on a Multi-head Deep Q-Network (MH-DQN) architecture with Prioritized Experience Replay (PER), has 17 distinct hyperparameters.
To manage this complexity, a three-round coarse-to-fine optimization strategy was adopted.
For all rounds, each trial consisted of 100 training episodes, each with a maximum of 100 steps, followed by 20 evaluation episodes.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item 
        In the initial round, the hyperparameters were organized into four logical groups: Learning Rate, Architecture, Replay Buffer, and Exploration Strategy.
        An independent search was conducted for each group over 50 trials, utilizing broad search spaces to identify promising regions.
    \item 
        In the second round, the search range for each hyperparameter was refined based on the initial results.
        Ranges were expanded for parameters whose optimal values were found at the boundaries of the search space and narrowed for those found centrally, allowing for a more granular search.
        This focused round was also conducted over 50 trials per group.
    \item 
        In the final round, a unified fine-tuning sweep was performed across all 17 hyperparameters simultaneously over 100 trials.
        This approach accounts for interdependencies between parameters from different groups.
        The search spaces were further narrowed based on the results from the second round to converge on the optimal configuration.
\end{enumerate}

The target network update rate, \texttt{RDC\_tau}, exhibits a clear pattern.
The vast majority of high-performing trials are concentrated at very low values, specifically in the range of [0,0.02].

As \texttt{tau} increases, the objective values become significantly more varied and trend lower.

The \texttt{RDC\_epsilon\_decay}, which controls the exploration decay of the $\epsilon$-Greedy strategy, reveals a distinct optimal region.
The best results are found for decay rates between approximately 0.96 and 0.985.
Values outside of this band, both lower and higher, correlate with more scattered and generally poorer outcomes.
This suggests that a carefully balanced exploration schedule is necessary for the agent to succeed.

Finally, the \texttt{RDC\_batch\_size} plot indicates a preference for medium-sized batches.
The most consistent high-performing trials are clustered at batch sizes of 128 and 256.
While good results are possible at other sizes, the smallest (64) and largest (1024) batches show much greater variance and are associated with many of the lowest-performing trials.

\subsection{User-Incentive Coordinator (UIC)}\label{sec:evaluation-ho-uic}
The UIC agent, which employs a Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm, has 15 distinct hyperparameters to be optimized.
A multi-stage optimization strategy, similar to that used for the RDC, was implemented over three rounds.
Each trial in every round was run for 50,000 timesteps.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item 
        The initial round identified promising search areas using broad hyperparameter ranges.
        For the first two rounds, the parameters were divided into three logical groups: PPO Core, Architecture, and Stability.
        The initial round consisted of 30 trials.
    \item 
        The second round refined the search by narrowing or expanding the parameter ranges based on the initial outcomes, following the same logic as the RDC optimization.
        The second round again consisted of 30 trials.
    \item 
        The final round optimized all 15 parameters simultaneously to fine-tune the configuration, thereby accounting for any cross-group parameter dependencies.
        To account for the increase in complexity, the final round consisted of 100 trials.
\end{enumerate}

The aggreagated optimization process is illustrated by \autoref{fig:ho-uic-history}.
Each blue point corresponds to an individual trial, and the solid red line tracks the best-performing trial of each run.
It clearly shows a benefit of doing such a three round hyperparameter optimization strategy.
UNlike the RDC, the UIC's optimization history displays a distinct step-wise improvement.
This step-wise improvement, indicated by the solid red line, suggests that the PPO agent is more sensitive to specific paramter configurations.
This phenomenom is likely due to the less manual optimization before running the HO process.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:evaluation-setup}
Fleet sizes for the experiments have been determined by the mean number of vehicles available based on the collected GBFS snapshots.
For training and hyperparameter optimization, this fleet size was reduced to 90\% to allow the agents to learn from more experiences with vehicle undersupply.
For the final evaluation, three different fleet sizes were used to test the robustness: the mean fleet size (100\%), an undersupply scenario (80\%), and an oversupply scenario (1290\%).
Evaluating the models under these varied conditions ensures a more comprehensive assessment of their performance in both oversupply and undersupply situations.


\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}\label{sec:evaluation-setup-metrics}
To quantitatively compare the performance of the different rebalancing strategies developed by HRL framework an by the baselines, the following metrics are used:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Satisfied Demand Ratio:} 
        This is the primary metric for evaluating the service quality and user satisfaction.
        This ratio is defined as the percentage of total user trip requests that were successfully served, as formulated in \autoref{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand-ratio}.
        A higher Satisfied Demand Ratio indicates a more effective rebalancing strategy.
    \item \textbf{Manual Rebalancing Costs:}
        This metric quantifies the operational cost of manual rebalancing actions.
        It is calculated as the total number of vehicles rebalanced by service workers in the whole evaluation period.
        A lower value is preferred.
    \item \textbf{User-based Rebalancing Costs:}
        This metric, on the other hand, measures the costs incurred by offering incentives to users for rebalancing vehicles on trip drop-off.
        It is calculated as the total number of trips where users accepted the offered incentive to change their drop-off location.
        Similarly to the previous metric, a lower value is also preferred.
    \item \textbf{Fleet Imbalance:}
        The Gini index is used as a metric to measure the equity of the vehicle distribution.
        This metric is calculated over the number of vehicles per zone at every time step and then averaged (see \autoref{eq:pb-objective-gini}).
        A Gini index of zero indicates a fully balanced fleet, and a value of one indicates the maximum imbalance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines}\label{sec:evaluation-setup-baselines}
To evaluate the proposed HRL rebalancing framework, multiple baselines have been selected.
The evaluation against these baselines elevates the interpretability of the results.
Each baseline has been selected to test a distinct hypothesis, thereby thoroughly evaluating the design choices of the proposed framework.

\subsubsection{RDC-only Baseline}
The first baseline for comparison is running the Regional Distribution Coordinator (RDC) only, without the User-Incentive Coordinators (UICs).
By comparing the results to this baseline, it can be observed whether broad, operator-based rebalancing is enough to significantly improve trip satisfaction.


\subsubsection{UIC-only Baseline}
The second baseline involves running only the second tier of the proposed HRL framework, i.e., only the UIC agents propose incentive levels to locally rebalance the system.
This baseline, which involves no rebalancing between communities, tests the hypothesis that localized, user-driven fleet optimization can achieve comparable results to a system with multi-level rebalancing.

\subsubsection{Greedy Heuristic}
The third baseline is a greedy heuristic designed to test whether the HRL approach can outperform a simple, rule-based algorithm. This baseline serves as a lower bound that the proposed framework should outperform. The greedy heuristic is implemented in the same two-tier hierarchy as the HRL framework:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Top-Tier (Operator Rebalancing):}
        The top-tier heuristic calculates the expected vehicle deficit for each community based on current vehicle counts and demand forecasts.
        It then prioritizes communities with the highest deficit or surplus.
        To meet the expected demand, it selects the most suitable discrete action from the same action space available to the RDC, with the total number of rebalanced vehicles capped at 5\% of the total fleet size per hour to simulate operational constraints.

    \item \textbf{Second-Tier (User Incentives):}
        The second-tier heuristic determines incentive levels for each zone within a community.
        It calculates the ratio of vehicle supply to expected demand for each zone.
        If this ratio is below a threshold of 0.8, a maximal incentive is offered to encourage users to drop-off vehicles in that zone.
        If the ratio exceeds the threshold or no demand is expected, no incentive is offered.
\end{itemize}
