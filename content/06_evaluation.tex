The following chapter details the experimental setup used for evaluation, the hyperparameter optimization for the \gls{rdc} and \gls{uic} agents, the analysis of the final results, and the discussion of these results.    


\section{Hyperparameter Optimization}\label{sec:evaluation-ho}
The performance of RL agents is highly dependent on the selection of their hyperparameters \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.
A systematic \gls{ho} is therefore essential to ensure agent efficacy, particularly for a complex \gls{hrl} framework such as the one proposed \cite{pateria_hierarchical_2021}.
A dedicated \gls{ho} process was conducted for both the \glsfirst{rdc} and the \glsfirst{uic} agents to maximize their individual performance.
This section outlines the optimization methodology and presents the final hyperparameter configurations used in the subsequent evaluation of the \gls{hrl} framework

The optimization objective for both agents was to maximize a composite reward function.
This function is a weighted sum of three metrics: maximizing satisfied user demand ($R_{demand}$), minimizing rebalancing costs ($R_{cost}$), and promoting equitable vehicle distribution ($R_{gini}$).\\

For the \gls{ho} process, the Optuna framework was utilized, a state-of-the-art optimization tool \cite{akiba_optuna_2019}.
The \gls{tpe} \cite{bergstra_algorithms_2011} was selected as the search algorithm due to its effectiveness in complex, high-dimensional search spaces \cite{akiba_optuna_2019}.
\gls{tpe} probabilistically models the search space to efficiently identify promising hyperparameter configurations.
The algorithm fits a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) to a set of well-performing parameter configurations and calculates a ratio against a GMM fitted to the remaining configurations.
The sampler then selects the parameter value that maximizes this ratio, guiding the search toward more promising regions \cite{bergstra_algorithms_2011}.

To ensure reproducibility, all optimization runs were seeded.
As \gls{ho} is computationally intensive, the process was parallelized across multiple jobs.
Furthermore, demand forecasts were pre-calculated to reduce computation time. The remainder of this section details the specific \gls{ho} processes for the \gls{rdc} and \gls{uic} agents.

\paragraph{Reward Weight Optimization}
Initial experiments in which the reward weights themselves were included in the \gls{ho} process consistently led to undesirable agent behaviors.
Both \gls{rdc} and \gls{uic} agents learned to adopt passive strategies, as avoiding rebalancing entirely was the most effective way to achieve a high reward under automatically discovered weight configurations.
This behavior directly conflicted with the intended operational goals.
To address this, the reward weights were chosen manually through targeted short-run experiments with different weight configurations.
This manual tuning ensured that the reward structure encouraged active rebalancing while balancing the trade-offs between demand satisfaction, operational costs, and fleet balance.
Furthermore, identical reward weights were selected for both the \gls{rdc} and \gls{uic} agents to align their objectives and prevent them from working in a counterproductive way during joint operation.
The final chosen weights are documented in \autoref{tab:rdc_final_hyperparameters} and \autoref{tab:uic_final_hyperparameters}.

\subsection{Regional Distribution Coordinator (RDC)}\label{sec:evaluation-ho-rdc}
The \gls{rdc} agent, based on a \gls{mh-dqn} architecture with \gls{per}, has 17 distinct hyperparameters.
To manage this complexity, a three-round coarse-to-fine optimization strategy was adopted.
For all rounds, each trial consisted of 100 training episodes, each with a maximum of 100 steps, followed by 20 evaluation episodes.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item 
        In the initial round, the hyperparameters were organized into four logical groups: Learning Rate, Architecture, Replay Buffer, and Exploration Strategy.
        An independent search was conducted for each group over 50 trials, utilizing broad search spaces to identify promising regions.
    \item 
        In the second round, the search range for each hyperparameter was refined based on the initial results.
        Ranges were expanded for parameters whose optimal values were found at the boundaries of the search space and narrowed for those found centrally, allowing for a more granular search.
        This focused round was also conducted over 50 trials per group.
    \item 
        In the final round, a unified fine-tuning sweep was performed across all 17 hyperparameters simultaneously over 100 trials.
        This approach accounts for interdependencies between parameters from different groups.
        The search spaces were further narrowed based on the results from the second round to converge on the optimal configuration.
\end{enumerate}

The results of the three-round optimization process are illustrated in \autoref{fig:ho-rdc-history}.
Each blue point corresponds to an individual trial, and the solid red line tracks the best-performing trial of each run.

\subsection{User-Incentive Coordinator (UIC)}\label{sec:evaluation-ho-uic}
The \gls{uic} agent, which employs a \gls{ppo} algorithm, has 15 distinct hyperparameters to be optimized.
A multi-stage optimization strategy, similar to that used for the \gls{rdc}, was implemented over three rounds.
Each trial in every round was run for 50,000 timesteps.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item 
        The initial round identified promising search areas using broad hyperparameter ranges.
        For the first two rounds, the parameters were divided into three logical groups: \gls{ppo} Core, Architecture, and Stability.
        The initial round consisted of 30 trials.
    \item 
        The second round refined the search by narrowing or expanding the parameter ranges based on the initial outcomes, following the same logic as the \gls{rdc} optimization.
        The second round again consisted of 30 trials.
    \item 
        The final round optimized all 15 parameters simultaneously to fine-tune the configuration, thereby accounting for any cross-group parameter dependencies.
        To account for the increase in complexity, the final round consisted of 100 trials.
\end{enumerate}

The aggregated optimization process is illustrated by \autoref{fig:ho-uic-history}.
Each blue point corresponds to an individual trial, and the solid red line tracks the best-performing trial of each run.
It clearly demonstrates the benefits of employing a three-round hyperparameter optimization strategy.
Unlike the \gls{rdc}, the \gls{uic}'s optimization history displays a step-wise improvement pattern, although the steps are less pronounced in the later stages.
This suggests that while the \gls{ppo} agent is sensitive to specific parameter configurations, gains beyond certain thresholds become increasingly incremental.
This phenomenon is likely due to the reduced amount of manual tuning before running the \gls{ho} process compared to the \gls{rdc}.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:evaluation-setup}
Fleet sizes for the experiments have been determined by the mean number of vehicles available based on the collected \gls{gbfs} snapshots.
For training and hyperparameter optimization, this fleet size was reduced to 90\% to allow the agents to learn from more experiences with vehicle undersupply.
For the final evaluation, three different fleet sizes were used to test the robustness: the mean fleet size (100\%), an undersupply scenario (67\%), and an oversupply scenario (133\%).
Evaluating the models under these varied conditions ensures a more comprehensive assessment of their performance in both oversupply and undersupply situations.


\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}\label{sec:evaluation-setup-metrics}
To quantitatively compare the performance of the different rebalancing strategies developed by the \gls{hrl} framework and the baselines, the following metrics are used:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\glsfirst{sdr}:} 
        The primary metric for evaluating the service quality and user satisfaction.
        This ratio is defined as the percentage of total user trip requests that were successfully served, as formulated in \autoref{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand-ratio}.
        A higher \gls{sdr} indicates a more effective rebalancing strategy.
    \item \textbf{Manual Rebalancing Costs:}
        This metric quantifies the operational cost of manual rebalancing actions.
        It is calculated as the total number of vehicles rebalanced by service workers in the whole evaluation period.
        A lower value is preferred.
    \item \textbf{User-based Rebalancing Costs:}
        This metric, on the other hand, measures the costs incurred by offering incentives to users for rebalancing vehicles on trip drop-off.
        It is calculated as the total number of trips where users accepted the offered incentive to change their drop-off location.
        Similarly to the previous metric, a lower value is also preferred.
    \item \textbf{Fleet Imbalance:}
        The Gini coefficient is used as a metric to measure the equity of the vehicle distribution \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025}.
        This metric is calculated over the number of vehicles per zone at every timestep and then averaged (see \autoref{eq:pb-objective-gini}).
        A Gini coefficient of zero indicates a fully balanced fleet, and a value of one indicates the maximum imbalance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines}\label{sec:evaluation-setup-baselines}
To evaluate the proposed \gls{hrl} rebalancing framework, multiple baselines have been selected.
The evaluation against these baselines elevates the interpretability of the results.
Each baseline has been selected to test a distinct hypothesis, thereby thoroughly evaluating the design choices of the proposed framework.

\subsubsection{RDC-only Baseline}
The first baseline for comparison is running the \gls{rdc} only, without the \glspl{uic}.
By comparing the results to this baseline, it can be observed whether broad, operator-based rebalancing is enough to significantly improve trip satisfaction.


\subsubsection{UIC-only Baseline}
The second baseline involves running only the second tier of the proposed \gls{hrl} framework, i.e., only the \gls{uic} agents propose incentive levels to locally rebalance the system.
This baseline, which involves no rebalancing between communities, tests the hypothesis that localized, user-driven fleet optimization can achieve comparable results to a system with multi-level rebalancing.

\subsubsection{Greedy Heuristic}
The third baseline is a greedy heuristic designed to test whether the \gls{hrl} approach can outperform a simple, rule-based algorithm. This baseline serves as a lower bound that the proposed framework should outperform. The greedy heuristic is implemented in the same two-tier hierarchy as the \gls{hrl} framework:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Top-Tier (Operator-based Rebalancing):}
        The top-tier heuristic calculates the expected vehicle deficit for each community based on current vehicle counts and demand forecasts.
        It then prioritizes communities with the highest deficit or surplus.
        To meet the expected demand, it selects the most suitable discrete action from the same action space available to the \gls{rdc}, with the total number of rebalanced vehicles capped at 5\% of the total fleet size per hour to simulate operational constraints.

    \item \textbf{Second-Tier (User-based Rebalancing):}
        The second-tier heuristic determines incentive levels for each zone within a community.
        It calculates the ratio of vehicle supply to expected demand for each zone.
        If this ratio is below a threshold of 0.8, a maximal incentive is offered to encourage users to drop-off vehicles in that zone.
        If the ratio exceeds the threshold or no demand is expected, no incentive is offered.
\end{itemize}

\section{Results}
This section presents the performance evaluation of the proposed \gls{hrl} framework.
The results are analyzed to validate the design choices of the hierarchical architecture and to understand the trade-offs between satisfying user demand, the associated costs of rebalancing, and fleet equity.

The experiments follow the design described in \autoref{sec:evaluation-setup}.
Each model–fleet size configuration was evaluated over 20 independent runs to account for stochasticity in the simulation environment and learning process.
The analysis covers all five models introduced in \autoref{sec:evaluation-setup-baselines} across the three fleet size scenarios: 600, 900, and 1200 vehicles.
Performance is assessed using the four evaluation metrics defined in \autoref{sec:evaluation-setup-metrics}.

\subsection{Metric-Specific Analysis}
Each evaluation metric is examined individually before considering cross-metric trade-offs.


\subsubsection{\glsfirst{sdr}}
The performance of the proposed \gls{hrl} framework and its baselines in terms of service quality is presented in \autoref{fig:eval-bars-sdr}, which illustrates the \glsfirst{sdr} across different fleet sizes.
The results of the proposed \gls{hrl} models show mixed results.
The \texttt{HRL Manual Only} model, representing the strategic \gls{rdc} agent acting alone, consistently achieves the highest SDR across all scenarios, satisfying approximately 84\% of user demand in the balanced 900-vehicle scenario.
The fully integrated \texttt{HRL Both Enabled} framework demonstrates worse performance, achieving the second-highest SDR of approximately 80\% in the same scenario and outperforming the \texttt{No Rebalancing} and \texttt{Greedy Heuristic} baselines only slightly in the undersupply and balanced fleet size scenarios.
This performance degradation suggests a negative interaction between the two hierarchical levels.
Rather than creating synergy, the tactical actions of the low-level \gls{uic} agents appear to interfere with the globally effective strategy of the high-level \gls{rdc}.
Notably, in the oversupply scenario, the addition of the \gls{uic} agents even results in poorer results than the \texttt{No Rebalancing} and \texttt{Greedy Heuristic} baselines.
The \texttt{HRL Incentive Only} model underperforms relative to other strategies, suggesting, within the framework, user-based incentives alone are insufficient to achieve a balanced state and may be more effective as a fine-tuning mechanism on top of a globally balanced system.

The performance ranking of the models remains mostly consistent across the different fleet sizes.
The error bars confirm that the \texttt{HRL Manual Only} models provide a statistically significant improvement over the \texttt{No Rebalancing} baseline, particularly in the undersupply scenario.
Ultimately, these results underscore the need for further improvement of the fully integrated HRL framework.
While the high-level agent is effective in isolation, the successful combination of both agents remains a significant challenge for future research.

\subsubsection{Gini Coefficient}
An analysis of the Gini coefficient, which measures fleet distribution equity, reinforces the theme of hierarchical interference, as shown in \autoref{fig:eval-bars-gini}.
The figure illustrates the impact of each rebalancing strategy on the Gini coefficient, where lower values indicate a more equitable distribution.
The \gls{hrl}-based models outperform the baselines, except the \texttt{HRL Incentive Only} model.
\texttt{HRL Manual Only} achieves the best equity overall.
In the 900-vehicle scenario, the \texttt{HRL Manual Only} model reduces the Gini coefficient to approximately 0.24, a 40\% improvement over the \texttt{No Rebalancing} baseline ($\sim 0.40$), demonstrating its effectiveness in creating a globally balanced fleet.

Notably, the fully integrated \texttt{HRL Both Enabled} framework performs nearly as well, with a Gini coefficient of just 0.26 in the same scenario.
This suggests that incorporating localized, user-based incentives from the \gls{uic} agents results in only a marginal increase in imbalance.
In contrast, the \texttt{HRL Incentive Only} model is significantly less effective than the integrated approach, underperforming the \texttt{No Rebalancing} baseline in the fleet size scenarios of 900 and 1200 vehicles.
It suggests that while the \gls{rdc} establishes a highly equitable foundation, the localized incentives offered by the \glspl{uic} introduce minor disruptions that slightly degrade this overall balance.
The goal of fine-tuning local supply appears to come at a small but consistent cost to global equity.
The performance rankings remain consistent across all fleet sizes, confirming the robustness of this collaborative behavior.

\section{Discussion}
Evaluating the proposed HRL framework revealed a counterintuitive dynamic between its hierarchical levels.
The primary finding of the experimental evaluation is that the fully integrated HRL framework did not outperform the RDC-only baseline.
The high-level RDC operating in isolation demonstrated superior performance across key metrics such as the Satisfied Demand Ratio (SDR) and fleet equity (Gini coefficient).
This outcome suggests that the current two-tier structure causes harmful interference instead of the intended cooperation.

A key insight from the development process is that the agents can adapt their strategies.
Training the UICs in a simulation environment with an active RDC improved the combined HRL framework's performance by 10-15\% compared to a version with separately trained agents.
However, these adapted agents showed slightly worse performance when evaluated in the UIC-only baseline.
This demonstrates that the low-level UIC agents can adapt their policies to dynamic conditions created by the RDC's strategic vehicle relocations.
Consequently, the performance decline in the integrated HRL framework arises not from a lack of ability to learn, but from a misalignment between the RDC's long-term strategy and the local optimizations of the UICs.

Evidence from the evaluation suggests that the low-level UICs introduce inefficiencies that the high-level RDC must then counteract.
For instance, in the balanced 900-vehicle scenario, the \texttt{HRL Both Enabled} model incurred a higher manual rebalancing cost (7.62 relocations/hour) for a lower SDR (79.8\%) compared to the \texttt{HRL Manual Only} model (6.87 relocations/hour for 84.4\% SDR).
This implies the RDC was spending additional resources to correct imbalances at least partially induced by the UICs' actions.
The core issue, therefore, is not a failure of individual agent learning but a structural limitation in the integration of their learned policies.

One potential factor contributing to the suboptimal performance of the UIC agents is their extensive state-action space.
The UICs are responsible for deciding whether to provide an incentive in each zone of their community, and if so, determining the appropriate incentive level.
With approximately 30-40 zones per community, the dimensionality of the state-action space for each UIC agent becomes substantial, a well-known challenge in RL \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.
One additional challenge for the UICs is accurately choosing the levels of incentives.
The agents must select an incentive high enough to motivate the desired number of users but not so high that it causes local oversupply.
The UICs cannot directly control how many vehicles should be rebalanced or to how many users the incentive is offered.
Consequently, if the selected incentive levels are excessively high, too many users may end their trips in the target zones, creating new imbalances.
Conversely, if the incentives are set too low, no users accept the offer, and no rebalancing occurs.
This fine-grained trade-off, which must be managed for each zone, proves to be particularly difficult.

One potential improvement would be to decrease the operational timestep for the UICs. Currently, the agents determine incentive levels for a 60-minute scope.
By reducing this interval to, for example, 15 minutes, the UICs could more accurately address imbalances within their respective communities.
This modification would result in more constructive interaction with the RDC.
Such a change better illustrates the agents' intended roles in the HRL framework: the RDC would manage long-term, strategic distribution, while the UICs would execute short-term, tactical adjustments for immediate gains.
