\section{Data}\label{sec:design-data}
The data underlying this thesis was collected from a General Bike Sharing Specification (GBFS) feed that publishes the live locations of e-scooters from sharing services operating in Baden-Württemberg \cite{mobidata_bw_gebundelte_2022}.
These services utilize a free-floating approach, permitting users to pick-up and drop-off vehicles at any location within the operating area, without restriction to specific parking stations.
This section details the collection, preprocessing, and exploratory analysis of this GBFS data used to develop such a strategy.


\subsection{Data Collection}
To capture a comprehensive view of e-scooter usage, data was acquired from the GBFS feeds of the two most prominent service providers in Stuttgart: Bolt and Voi.
The data was collected by crawling the public feed at 15-minute intervals, creating detailed time-series snapshots of all available vehicles.
This process was conducted continuously over a 128-day period, from 11 February 2025 to 18 June 2025.

The GBFS format, a standard for publishing shared micromobility vehicle data, provides several key attributes for each vehicle.
These include its unique identifier, GPS coordinates (latitude and longitude), operational status (reserved or disabled), and battery level.
A complete list of the data fields extracted from the feed is presented in \autoref{tab:gbfs_fields}.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}
The primary goal of preprocessing was to transform the raw location snapshots into a dataset of discrete pick-up and drop-off events.
A methodological limitation of the GBFS feed is the randomization of vehicle identifiers for user anonymization, which prevents the direct tracking of individual trips.
To overcome this, an inferential approach was adopted: by comparing consecutive snapshots, the disappearance of a vehicle ID from one frame to the next was classified as a pick-up, while the appearance of a new ID was classified as a drop-off.

This event inference process yielded a total of 2,168,331 combined pick-up and drop-off events for Voi and 680,459 for Bolt.
Subsequently, the dataset was filtered to remove geographical outliers by defining a boundary corresponding to the city of Stuttgart and excluding events in outlying regions (e.g., Böblingen).
This filtering had a notable effect on the Voi dataset, reducing it by 59.6\% to 876,511 events.
In contrast, the Bolt dataset was largely unaffected, retaining 96.4\% of its data with 656,082 events remaining.
This disparity suggests a differing operational focus between the two providers, with Voi maintaining a much larger service presence in the suburbs surrounding Stuttgart.

Finally, the cleaned event data was aggregated to create a structured dataset for model training.
For spatial aggregation, a two-layer hierarchical approach was implemented using the H3 hexagonal grid system \cite{uber_h3_2018}.
The finest spatial unit, a \textit{zone}, was defined using H3 resolution 8, resulting in 291 distinct zones across the study area.
These zones were then grouped into larger \textit{communities} at H3 resolution 6, producing a total of 9 communities.
This hierarchical structure allows for analysis at both a granular and a regional level.
Temporally, events within each zone were aggregated into one-hour time bins.

This H3 system was chosen for three key reasons.
First, its hierarchical structure facilitates seamless aggregation at different spatial resolutions, which is ideal for the community-zone model used in this thesis.
Second, hexagons provide uniform distances to all six neighbors, ensuring greater consistency in spatial analysis compared to square grids.
Third, as a global standard, H3 ensures that the grid is predefined worldwide, simplifying the potential application of this methodology to other cities.

\section{Problem Definition}\label{sec:design-problem_definition}
This section formalizes the fleet rebalancing problem of a free-floating e-scooter sharing system.
The problem definition considers both operator-based rebalancing, performed by service workers, and user-based rebalancing, relocations performed by users guided by incentives.
The rebalancing mechanisms addressed are operator-based relocations between communities and user-based relocations within communities, which are achieved through incentives.
The routing problem of service vehicles performing operator-based rebalancing is not addressed in this work. 
Several works have been published on the routing problem for operator-based rebalancing, so there is no need to for further research \cite{lee_battery_2024, emami_integrated_2024, jimenez-merono_optimization_2024, paparella_optimization-based_2023, sanchez_simulation_2022}.  
The state-of-charge (SOC) of the vehicles isn't modeled in the simulation as well, this is due to the limited data availability and this has been done in multiple recent works as well \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025, villa-zapata_incorporating_2024, schofield_handling_2021, pan_deep_2019}.
As detailed in \autoref{sec:design-data} only the trip start and endpoints are available and no full trip data.
Therefore, modeling whole trips in the simulation, which is nesecarry to update the SOC accordingly, isn't directly possible with the available data.
In \autoref{sec:conclusion-future_work} possible solutions to incorporate the SOC into the simulation are dicussed.

The time is discretized into hourly intervals ($\mathcal{T}$) in which all pick-up, drop-off, and rebalancing events occur.
The fleet size ($\mathcal{N}$) remains constant over all timesteps $t$.
\autoref{tab:problem_notation} summarizes all notation used for the problem formulation, including all sets, parameters, and decision variables.
The subset of zones for a given community $c$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{Z}_c = \{z \in \mathcal{Z} \mid \kappa(z) = c\}
\end{equation}

\subsection{State Variables}
The formulated rebalancing problem distinguishes between forecasted demand ($\hat P_z(t)$, $\hat D_z(t)$), which forms part of the state representation used for decision-making, and actual demand ($P_z(t)$, $P_z(t)$), which drives the simulation used for training and evaluation.
All performance metrics, such as the satisfied demand, are computed against the actual demand values.

\subsection{Rebalancing actions}
The framework considers both operator-based and user-based rebalancing, which is why the problem definition includes decision variables for both approaches.
Those decision variables are described in the following paragraphs.
These decision variables represent the outcomes of rebalancing efforts.
They do not directly correspond to the action spaces of the reinforcement learning agents, which are detailed in \autoref{sec:design-rl_framework}.

\subsubsection{Operator-based rebalancing}
Operator-based rebalancing is performed to relocate e-scooters between communities.
Service workers move e-scooters between communities, which is represented by $X_{c\rightarrow c'}(t)$, describing the number of vehicles relocated from community $c$ to community $c'$ at time step $t$.

\subsubsection{User-based rebalancing}
User-based rebalancing is performed between zones inside a community.
Users are incentivized to drop-off vehicles at a different zone than they originally intended to.
The decision variable $Y_{z\rightarrow z'}(t)$ describes such a relocation done by a user from zone $z$ to $z'$.
\autoref{eq:rb-user-constraint-spatial} constrains user-based rebalancing to occur only within a community.
Additionally, user-based rebalancing operations are restricted to the neighboring zones.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rb-user-constraint-spatial}
Y_{z\to z'}(t) \;=\; 0
\quad
\forall\,t\in\mathcal{T},\;\forall\,z,z'\in\mathcal{Z}
\text{ such that }
\bigl(A_{z,z'}=0\bigr)\;\lor\;\bigl(\kappa(z)\neq\kappa(z')\bigr).
\end{equation}

\subsection{System Dynamics}
 

\autoref{eq:rp-fleet-conservation} formulates the global fleet conservation.
This constraint ensures that the total number of e-scooters across all zones remains constant at every time period $t$.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:rp-fleet-conservation}
    \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}}n_z(t) = \mathcal{N}, \forall t\in \mathcal{T}
\end{equation}


\autoref{eq:pb-demand-flow} illustrates the demand flow of a single zone, defining the number of e-scooters at time $t+1$ by accounting for demand and relocation operations.
This demand flow enables a step-wise calculation of available scooters at every timestep $t$.
The demand flow considers both pick-up demand $P_z(t)$ and drop-off demand $D_z(t)$.
Additionally, rebalancing operations are considered by the decision variables $X_{c\rightarrow c'}(t)$ and $Y_{z\rightarrow z'}(t)$.
The formulation assumes that vehicles moved by operators to a community $c'$ are distributed uniformly among all zones $\mathcal{Z}_{\kappa(z)}$ within that community.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:pb-demand-flow}
    \begin{aligned}
        n_z(t+1) =\;&\ n_z(t) - P_z(t) + D_z(t) \\
        &+  \sum_{c' \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Z}_{\kappa(z)}|} X_{c'\rightarrow \kappa(z)}(t) - \sum_{c' \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Z}_{\kappa(z)}|} X_{\kappa(z)\rightarrow c'}(t) \\
        &+ \sum_{z' \in \mathcal{Z}} Y_{z'\rightarrow z}(t) - \sum_{z' \in \mathcal{Z}} Y_{z\rightarrow z'}(t)
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Objective}

\autoref{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand} defines the satisfied demand, which is the demand that the service could satisfy with enough vehicles to serve the wanted pick-up demand.
This satisfied demand is used to calculate the ratio of satisfied demand, which defines the percentage of the asked demand to the amount of demand that could be satisfied by the system (see \autoref{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand-ratio}).

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand}
        S_z(t) = \min\{P_z(t), n_z(t) + D_z(t)\}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand-ratio}
        r^S_z(t) = 
        \begin{cases}
            \frac{S_z(t)}{P_z(t)}, & P_z(t) > 0,\\[8pt]
            0, & P_z(t) = 0.
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
\end{subequations}

\autoref{eq:pb-objective} defines the objective of the rebalancing problem.
It combines the costs from rebalancing operations performed by user- and operator-based rebalancing, and includes minimizing the amount of unmet demand.
The objective is calculated over the whole simulation horizon, e.g., over all time steps $t$.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:pb-objective-gini}
G(t) \;=\; 
\frac{1}{2 \, \mathcal{N}_z^2 \, \bar{n}(t)} 
\sum_{i=1}^{\mathcal{N}_z} \sum_{j=1}^{\mathcal{N}_z} 
\bigl\lvert n_i(t) - n_j(t)\bigr\rvert,
\qquad
\bar{n}(t) \;=\; \frac{1}{\mathcal{N}_z} \sum_{z=1}^{\mathcal{N}_z} n_z(t).
\end{equation}

Similarly, the Gini coefficient for a single community $c$, used to measure intra-community imbalance, is defined as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pb-objective-gini-community}
G_c(t) \;=\; 
\frac{1}{2 \, |\mathcal{Z}_c|^2 \, \bar{n}_c(t)} 
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{Z}_c} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{Z}_c} 
\bigl\lvert n_i(t) - n_j(t)\bigr\rvert,
\qquad
\bar{n}_c(t) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Z}_c|} \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}_c} n_z(t).
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:pb-objective}
\max\;\sum_{t\in \mathcal{T}}\Biggl[
  \sum_{z\in \mathcal{Z}} r^S_z(t)
  \;+\;(1 - \frac{\sum_{c,c' \in \mathcal{C}} X_{c\to c'}(t)}{\mathcal{N}})
  \;+\;(1 - \frac{\sum_{z,z' \in \mathcal{Z}} Y_{z\to z'}(t)}{\mathcal{N}})
  \;+\;(1 - G(t))
\Biggr].
\end{equation}


This problem can be modeled using two coupled Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), where the rebalancing outcomes $X_{c\rightarrow c'}(t)$ and $Y_{z\rightarrow z'}(t)$ result from the actions taken within a two-tier hierarchical reinforcement learning framework.
The objective of this problem formulation can be seen as the reward function for both agents.
The following section defines an HRL framework that manages operator-based rebalancing on a macroscopic level and user-based rebalancing on a microscopic level.

 \section{Framework}\label{sec:design-rl_framework}
A \gls{HRL} framework is proposed to address the e-scooter rebalancing challenge.
The HRL framework comprises two tiers.
The first tier, the \gls{RDC}, manages inter-community imbalances by determining the target number of vehicles for each defined community.
These imbalances are addressed through operator-based rebalancing, where service workers collect vehicles from communities with an oversupply and redistribute them to communities experiencing an undersupply.
The second tier consists of multiple \gls{UICs}, each assigned to a specific community, to manage intra-community imbalances (i.e., imbalances within a single community).
These coordinators aim to promote an equal distribution of vehicles by offering incentives to users for returning vehicles to designated undersupplied zones at the end of their trips.
\autoref{fig:hrl-architecture} illustrates the architecture of this two-tier \gls{HRL} framework.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.9\linewidth]{content/figures/hrl_architecture.pdf}
    \caption{Conceptual illustration of the two-tier Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning framework for e-scooter rebalancing.}
    \label{fig:hrl-architecture}
\end{figure}

The hierarchical approach has been chosen due to its ability to decompose a large problem, such as the rebalancing problem of an e-scooter sharing service, into more manageable sub-problems.
In this \gls{HRL} framework, the rebalancing problem is decomposed into sub-problems representing different spatial and operational levels.
The Regional Distribution Coordinator addresses the rebalancing problem on a macroscopic community level, while the Use-Incentive Coordinator handles it on a microscopic zone level.

A dedicated demand forecasting module supports both the Regional Distribution Coordinator and the User-Incentive Coordinators.
This module provides demand predictions as part of the state for both tiers, enhancing the effectiveness of rebalancing operations by allowing the system to anticipate future demand.
This modular design ensures that both tiers optimize rebalancing decisions based on consistent future outlooks and separates the demand forecasting task from the RL agents.
Additionally, the current vehicle distribution across all zones is provided as input to both agents. 



\subsection{Regional Distribution Coordinator}\label{sec:design-rl_framework-rdc}
The Regional Distribution Coordinator (RDC) constitutes the first tier of the proposed two-tier Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) framework.
Its primary purpose is to address inter-community imbalances by determining the target number of e-scooters for each defined community, facilitating operator-based rebalancing.

\subsubsection{Markov Decision Process (MDP) formulation}
The RDC is modeled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), defined by a tuple $(\mathcal{S, A, P, R})$, where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space, $\mathcal{A}$ is the action space, $\mathcal{P}$ is the state transition probability function, and $\mathcal{R}$ is the reward function \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.
Due to the complex dynamics of shared e-scooter systems, the transition probability function $\mathcal{P}$ is learned implicitly by the agent through interaction with the simulation environment.
The MDP is assumed to be stationary, meaning that the transition probabilities depend only on the current state and the selected action and not on the specific timestep $t$.
This MDP formulation is illustrated in \autoref{fig:rdc_mdp}.

\paragraph{State ($\mathcal{S}^{RDC}_t$)}
The state representation for the RDC at timestep $t$, denoted $\mathcal{S}^{RDC}_t$, is a triple containing community-level vehicle counts ($n_c(t)$), pick-up demand forecast ($\hat{P}_c(t)$), and drop-off demand forecast ($\hat{D}_c(t)$).
These demand forecasts are generated by a separate demand forecasting module, detailed in \autoref{sec:design-rl_framework-forecasting_module}.


\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Community-level Vehicle Vounts ($n_c(t)$):}
    The current number of available e-scooters in the community $c$ at timestep $t$.
    \item \textbf{Pick-up Demand Forecast ($\hat{P}_c(t)$):}
    The forecasted number of e-scooter trips expected to originate from community $c$ during the next time interval ($t \to t+1$).
    \item \textbf{Drop-off Demand Forecast ($\hat{D}_c(t)$):}
    The forecasted number of e-scooter trips expected to end in community $c$ during the next time interval ($t \to t+1$).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Action ($\mathcal{A}^{RDC}_t$)}
The RDC's action at timestep $t$, $\mathcal{A}^{RDC}_t$, is a vector $[a_1(t), a_2(t), ..., a_{N_{\mathcal{C}}}(t)]$, where $N_{\mathcal{C}}$ is the total number of communities.
Each component $a_c(t)$ specifies the rebalancing quota for community $c$, representing the target number of e-scooters to be added to (positive value) or removed from (negative value) that community.
The action space for each component $a_c(t)$ is discrete and identical across communities. For this study, the action space for each $a_c(t)$ is defined as $\{-15, -10, -5, 0, 5, 10, 15\}$, providing seven distinct rebalancing options per community.

The RDC aims to select actions that balance supply across all communities. While ideal actions might inherently preserve the overall fleet size, the RDC can select a set of actions that, if summed, would change the total number of vehicles.
Such scenarios are managed by the operator-rebalancing algorithms within the simulation environment, which execute the rebalancing operations. If necessary, the simulator scales the raw action values (quotas) proportionally to ensure that the total fleet size remains constant, thereby preserving the RDC's intended rebalancing direction while sticking to operational constraints.

\paragraph{Reward ($\mathcal{R}^{RDC}_t$)}
With state and action now defined, next specify how the RDC quantifies its performance via the reward function.
The reward function of the RDC is designed to guide the agent towards effective rebalancing strategies.
It is calculated after the execution of the rebalancing action $\mathcal{A}^{RDC}_t$ and the simulation of user demand flow for the current timestep. The total reward $\mathcal{R}^{RDC}_t$ is a weighted sum of three components.
These components reflect the primary operational goals: maximizing service utility, minimizing operational costs, and ensuring equitable vehicle availability.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:rdc-reward}
    \mathcal{R}^{RDC}_t = \frac{w_{demand}^{RDC} \cdot R_{demand}^{RDC}(t) + w_{cost}^{RDC} \cdot R_{cost}^{RDC}(t) + w_{gini}^{RDC} \cdot R_{gini}^{RDC}(t)}{w_{demand}^{RDC}+ w_{cost}^{RDC}+ w_{gini}^{RDC}}
\end{equation}
where $w_{demand}^{RDC}$, $w_{cost}^{RDC}$, and $w_{gini}^{RDC}$ are non-negative weighting factors. Each component reward ($R_{demand}^{RDC}(t)$, $R_{cost}^{RDC}(t)$, $R_{gini}^{RDC}(t)$) is individually normalized to a range of [0, 1].
The specific values for the weighting factors and the methodology for their selection are detailed in \autoref{sec:implementation_rdc}.

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rdc-reward-demand}
        R_{demand}^{RDC}(t) = \frac{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}}S_z(t)}{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}}D_z(t)}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rdc-reward-cost}
        R_{cost}^{RDC}(t) = 1 -  \left(\frac{\sum_{c, c' \in \mathcal{C}}X_{c \to c'}(t)}{\mathcal{N}}\right)^2
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rdc-reward-gini}
        R_{gini}^{RDC}(t) = 1 - G(t)
    \end{equation}
\end{subequations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Satisfied Demand Ratio ($R_{demand}^{RDC}(t)$):}
    This core component quantifies the effectiveness of rebalancing actions by measuring the proportion of satisfied trip requests.
    It is calculated by summing, over all communities, the number of trip requests the system successfully served, and dividing this sum by the total number of trip requests received during the current time interval (see \autoref{eq:rdc-reward-demand}).
    This normalization results in a stable reward signal between 0 and 1.
    \item \textbf{Rebalancing Cost ($R_{cost}^{RDC}(t)$):}
    To penalize excessive rebalancing, this component incorporates the cost of rebalancing operations.
    It is calculated by summing the total number of vehicles rebalanced (picked up or dropped off by operators) in the current timestep.
    This sum is then divided by the total fleet size to receive a normalized rebalancing ratio.
    This ratio is squared and then transformed to ensure that a higher number of rebalancing operations results in a lower value for this reward component, and vice versa (see \autoref{eq:rdc-reward-cost}).
    \item \textbf{Imbalance Penalty ($R_{gini}^{RDC}(t)$):}
    This component encourages an equitable distribution of the e-scooter fleet across communities.
    It is derived from the Gini coefficient, a measure of statistical dispersion, calculated over the vehicle counts in all communities.
    The Gini coefficient itself ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality).
    This value is then transformed to align with the reward structure (see \autoref{eq:rdc-reward-gini}), resulting in a normalized component that is high when the e-scooter fleet is evenly distributed and low for an imbalanced distribution \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Algorithm and Architecture}
Given this MDP formulation, a Deep Q‐Network is implemented to approximate the optimal policy.
A multi-head Deep Q-Network (DQN) is employed for the Regional Distribution Coordinator. DQN is a value-based reinforcement learning algorithm that utilizes deep neural networks to approximate the optimal action-value function, $Q*(s, a)$ \cite{mnih_human-level_2015}.
The multi-head variant \cite{li_personalized_2024} is particularly suitable for the RDC as it needs to output multiple, correlated rebalancing actions (one for each community) simultaneously.
Alternative architectures, such as a single network predicting a composite action vector or Q-values for all joint actions, were considered but deemed impractical due to the combinatorial explosion of the action space.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{content/figures/mh-dqn-architecture.pdf}
    \caption{Multi‐head DQN architecture for the RDC, with shared layers and per‐community heads.}
    \label{fig:mdh-architecture}
\end{figure}

The core architecture of the multi-head DQN comprises three main types of layers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Input Layer:}
    Processes the state representation ($\mathcal{S}^{RDC}_t$), involving flattening and normalization of the input features (community-level vehicle counts and demand forecasts).
    \item \textbf{Shared Layers:}
    These layers receive input from the Input Layer and learn to extract features representing system-wide metrics and relationships relevant to all communities.
    \item \textbf{Head-Specific Layers:}
    A separate set of neural network layers (a \textit{head}) is dedicated to each community.
    Each head takes the processed features from the Shared Layers and outputs the Q-values for each discrete rebalancing action $a_c(t)$ applicable to its corresponding community.
    The action selected for a community is the one with the highest Q-value from its respective head.
\end{enumerate}
Each head thus determines the optimal rebalancing quota for its assigned community based on the global system state and learned rebalancing policies.

\paragraph{Prioritized Experience Replay (PER)}
The multi-head DQN utilizes Prioritized Experience Replay (PER) \cite{schaul_prioritized_2016} to enhance learning efficiency.
Standard DQN stores transitions $(s_t, a_t, r_t, s_{t+1})$ in a replay buffer and samples them uniformly for training.
With PER, transitions are sampled non-uniformly based on their significance, measured by the magnitude of the Temporal Difference (TD) error.
Prioritizing transitions with high TD-error, which are transitions where the agent learns the most, accelerates the learning process and improves sample efficiency.
However, this prioritization introduces a bias by modifying the distribution of training data.

This bias is mitigated using Importance Sampling (IS) weights, which adjust the contribution of each sampled transition during the learning updates.
These IS-weights are modulated by an exponent $\beta_{PER}$, which is annealed from an initial value towards one over the course of training (see \autoref{sec:implementation_rdc} for the exact annealing schedule).
This annealing schedule for $\beta_{PER}$ helps to balance the benefits of prioritized sampling with the need for unbiased updates, contributing to stable and efficient learning \cite{schaul_prioritized_2016}.

\paragraph{Exploration Strategy}
To ensure sufficient exploration of the state-action space, the RDC employs an $\epsilon$-greedy strategy.
In $\epsilon$-greedy, $\epsilon$ represents the probability of choosing a random action rather than the action considered optimal by the current policy.
To encourage broad exploration initially and gradually shift towards exploitation, $\epsilon$ is annealed over the course of the training process (see \autoref{sec:implementation_rdc} for the exact $\epsilon$ schedule).
It starts at a high value at the beginning of training and is reduced to a small, non-zero value by the end of training.
Maintaining a small $\epsilon$ ensures persistent exploration, which can be beneficial in complex and dynamic environments \cite{zhang_why_2020}.

\paragraph{Training stabilization techniques}
Several techniques are incorporated to stabilize the training of the DQN.
A separate target network, with an identical architecture to the policy network (the network being actively trained), is used to provide stable targets for updating the Q-value.
The target network's weights are not updated through backpropagation directly.
Instead, they are periodically updated using soft updates, where its weights ($\theta_{target}$) become a weighted average of its own current weights and the policy network's weights ($\theta_{policy}$): $\theta_{target} \leftarrow \tau \theta_{policy} + (1-\tau)\theta_{target}$, for a small $\tau$ (see \autoref{sec:implementation_rdc} for hyperparameter values).
This combination of a separate target network and soft updates contributes to stabilizing the training process.

To address the bias in Q-learning, where Q-values are frequently overestimated, a Double DQN (DDQN) algorithm \cite{hasselt_deep_2016} is integrated.
DDQN decouples action selection from action evaluation for the next state's value estimation.
Specifically, the policy network determines the optimal action for the next state, and the target network then evaluates the Q-value of this selected action.

Furthermore, to enhance performance and maintain training stability, a learning rate schedule is employed.
The learning rate starts higher to accelerate initial learning and is gradually decreased to facilitate fine-tuning and convergence to more optimal solutions.
Additionally, gradient clipping is applied during backpropagation to prevent exploding gradients, a common issue that can destabilize the training of deep neural networks \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.

\subsection{User-Incentive Coordinator}\label{sec:design-rl_framework-uic}
The second tier of the proposed HRL framework comprises multiple User-Incentive Coordinators (UIC), each assigned to a specific community to manage intra-community imbalances.
Intra-community imbalances are addressed through user-based rebalancing.
The UIC agents propose incentives for each zone that is part of the designated community to encourage users to drop-off vehicles at the end of their journey at zones that experience an undersupply of vehicles.


\subsubsection{Markov Decision Process (MDP) formulation}
The UIC's sequential decision-making problem is formalized as an MDP.
A diagram of this formulation is provided in \autoref{fig:uic_mdp}.

\paragraph{State ($\mathcal{S}^{UIC}_t$)}
The state representation for the UIC at timestep $t$, denoted as $\mathcal{S}^{UIC}_t$, is a triple containing zone level vehicle counts ($n_z(t)$), pick-up demand forecast ($\hat{P}_z(t)$), and drop-off demand forecast ($\hat{D}_z(t)$).
The state representation of the UIC is similarly defined as the state space of the RDC, but scoped to the specific community to which the UIC is designated.
The state representation consists of three components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Zone-level Vehicle Counts ($n_z(t)$):}
    The current number of available e-scooters in the zone $z$ at the current time step $t$. Where the community $\kappa(z)$ is the designated community of the UIC.
    \item \textbf{Pick-up Demand Forecast ($\hat{P}_z(t)$):}
    The forecasted number of e-scooters that are expected to originate at zone $z$ during the next time interval ($t \to t+1$). Where the community $\kappa(z)$ is the designated community of the UIC.
    \item \textbf{Drop-off demand forecast ($\hat{D}_z(t)$):}
    The forecasted number of e-scooters that are expected to be dropped off by users, e.g., the number of trips that are ending at zone $z$ in the next time interval ($t \to t+1$). Where the community $\kappa(z)$ is the designated community of the UIC.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Action ($\mathcal{A}^{UIC}_t$)}
The UIC's action space is continuous.
The UIC's actions represent continuous incentive levels between $[0,1]$ for each zone $z$ that is part of the designated community $c$.
This results in the following definition of the Actions:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A}^{UIC}_{t, c} = [a_1^{UIC}(t), ..., a_{N_{\mathcal{Z}_c}}^{UIC}(t)]
\end{equation}

$N_{\mathcal{Z}_c}$ defines the number of zones in community $c$.



\paragraph{Reward ($\mathcal{R}^{UIC}_t$)}

The reward function $\mathcal{R}^{UIC}_t$ of the UIC considers three components, each weighted by a non-negative weighting factor.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:uic-reward}
    \mathcal{R}^{UIC}_t(c) = \frac{w_{demand}^{UIC} \cdot R_{demand}^{UIC}(t, c) + w_{cost}^{UIC} \cdot R_{cost}^{UIC}(t, c) + w_{gini}^{UIC} \cdot R_{gini}^{UIC}(t, c)}{w_{demand}^{UIC}+ w_{cost}^{UIC}+ w_{gini}^{UIC}}
\end{equation}
where $w_{demand}^{UIC}$, $w_{cost}^{UIC}$, and $w_{gini}^{UIC}$ are non-negative weighting factors. Each component reward ($R_{demand}^{UIC}(t)$, $R_{cost}^{UIC}(t)$, $R_{gini}^{UIC}(t)$) is individually normalized to a range of [0, 1].
The specific values for the weighting factors and the methodology for their selection are detailed in \autoref{sec:implementation_uic}.

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:uic-reward-demand}
        R_{demand}^{UIC}(t, c) = \frac{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}_c}S_z(t)}{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}_c}D_z(t)}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:uic-reward-cost}
        R_{cost}^{UIC}(t, c) = 1 -  \left(\frac{\sum_{z, z' \in \mathcal{Z}_c}Y_{z \to z'}(t)}{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}_c}n_z(t)}\right)^2
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:uic-reward-gini}
        R_{gini}^{UIC}(t, c) = 1 - G_c(t)
    \end{equation}
\end{subequations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Satisfied Demand Ratio ($R_{demand}^{UIC}(t, c)$):}
    The core component of the reward function is the satisfied demand ratio, which measures the proportion of satisfied trip requests.
    It is calculated by summing the number of trip requests happening inside the designated community the system successfully served, and dividing this sum by the total number of trip requests received for each zone that is part of the community (see \autoref{eq:uic-reward-demand}).
    This Satisfied Demand Ratio is calculated for the time interval $[t, t+1]$. 
    
    \item \textbf{Rebalancing Cost ($R_{cost}^{UIC}(t, c)$):}
    Rebalancing operations negatively change the reward signal to encourage the UIC to limit rebalancing operations to only necessary operations.
    This Rebalancing Cost component is calculated by summing the number of vehicles rebalanced through user incentives inside the corresponding community, which is divided by the current number of vehicles present in this community.
    This results in a normalized ratio of possible rebalancing operations.
    This ratio is squared and inverted to penalize high numbers of rebalancing operations (see \autoref{eq:uic-reward-cost}).
    A low number of rebalancing operations results in higher values for this reward component.
    
    \item \textbf{Imbalance Penalty ($R_{gini}^{UIC}(t, c)$):}
    The last component of the reward signal of the UIC encourages an equitable distribution of the e-scooter fleet inside the community \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025}.
    It is calculated based on the Gini coefficient calculated over the vehicle count in all zones part of the designated community.
    
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Data Flow}
The UIC agent interacts with a simulated environment of the e-scooter sharing system.
This models the complex system dynamics of such sharing systems in a scoped and simplified manner for training performance reasons.

Inputs to the UICs environment contain the forecasted pick-up and drop-off demands for all zones inside the community.
These forecasted demands come from the Demand Forecasting Module which is detailed in \autoref{sec:design-rl_framework-forecasting_module}.
The real-time vehicle counts per zone are part of the environment system state.

Output from the UIC agent is a vector of continuous values for each zone in the range [0.0, 1.0] representing the incentive intensity for each zone part of the designated community.
These normalized incentive intensities are handled by a user response model part of the environment.
This model is defined by a user willingness function (\texttt{user\_willingness\_fn}).
This function maps incentive intensity values to a user's willingness factor.
This factor represents the fraction of users who accept the offered incentive.
In the simulation, the neighboring zones of the anticipated drop-off zone are identified, and the corresponding incentive intensities are derived from the selected action from the UIC.
From these neighboring zones, the zone with the highest incentive value is picked, and the user's willingness is calculated to update the drop-off demand for the current timestep.
This model represents a rational user choice based purely on the highest local incentive offered.
This \texttt{user\_willingness\_fn} is a simplifying assumption that models complex human behaviour.
This simplification is necessary for the simulation environment.

\subsubsection{Algorithm and Architecture}
A Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)\cite{schulman_proximal_2017} algorithm is implemented for the User-Incentive Coordinators.
The selection of PPO is made, as it can effectively handle continuous action spaces required for fine-grained setting of incentive intensities.

This algorithm allocates incentives to specific zones within a community to encourage users to drop-off vehicles in these targeted zones rather than their originally intended destinations.
The implementation leverages the Stable Baselines3 library, which provides a robust and stable implementation of the PPO algorithm \cite{raffin_stable-baselines3_2021}.
The PPO algorithm is an on-policy actor-critic method recognized for its stability and robust performance.
PPO is a policy gradient method which has been further discussed in \autoref{sec:background-reinforcement_learning-pgm}.

The implementation uses a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) architecture with separate networks for the policy (actor) and value (critic) functions.
Utilizing separate networks can improve stability by preventing the learning objectives of the policy and value functions from interfering with each other's gradients.
By isolating the actor and the critic into two distinct models, each network is updated with its own loss, thereby eliminating inferences between the two loss signals.
Both networks consist of three hidden layers, using the same network parameters, which are described with other implementation details in \autoref{sec:implementation_uic}.
For each zone in its surrounding community, the actor network outputs the mean and standard deviation parameters of a Gaussian distribution.
The continuous incentive for each zone is then sampled from the resulting distribution.
This sampling from a Gaussian distribution naturally results in exploration.
The critic network, on the other hand, is used for calculating the advantage of each action.
Therefore, the critic network outputs a single scalar value representing the estimate of the state-value function.
This estimate serves as a baseline to compute the advantage for each action.
The architecture is illustrated in \autoref{fig:ppo-architecture}.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{content/figures/ppo-architecture.pdf}
    \caption{PPO architecture for the UIC, with two networks for the actor and the critic.}
    \label{fig:ppo-architecture}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Exploration Strategy}
In contrast to the $\epsilon$-greedy strategy used for the RDCs MH-DQN agent, the PPO algorithm with a continuous action space has a stochastic nature, which results in some kind of exploration.
The degree of exploration is controlled by an entropy bonus, weighted by a coefficient.
This entropy bonus is added to the loss function to encourage the policy to maintain a higher randomness, preventing the model from converging to a suboptimal solution.

\paragraph{Training stabilization techniques}
PPO employs a Clipped Surrogate Objective function that ensures stable policy updates.
This mechanism constrains large policy updates, which potentially destabilize those.
The constraint is controlled by a clip range parameter, which is annealed over the training process.
Therefore, larger exploratory updates at the beginning of the training process are allowed; gradually decreasing the value of this parameter results in smaller and finer granular updates at the end of the training process.

Additionally, to further stabilize training, Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) is used.
GAE computes a weighted average of the advantage estimates over multiple timesteps, rather than relying on a single-step advantage value.
This technique, therefore, produces more stable estimates of the advantage function, resulting in faster learning and more reliable policy gradients. 

\subsection{Demand Forecasting Module}\label{sec:design-rl_framework-forecasting_module}
The successful operation of the rebalancing algorithm is fundamentally dependent on its ability to anticipate future demand to proactively rebalance vehicles based on future demand.
Therefore, a dedicated Demand Forecasting Module that accurately predicts future demand is critical.
By predicting future pick-up and drop-off events, this module enables the RL agents to pre-position vehicles to meet anticipated demand.
This results in a proactive rebalancing strategy that can respond actively to changes in demand.
This implementation of an independent Demand Forecasting Module removes the forecasting problem from the rebalancing algorithms themselves, which ideally should increase the overall efficiency of the framework, as the problems are separated and can be targeted directly.

To effectively support the RL rebalancing agents mentioned earlier, the Demand Forecasting Module must predict the number of pick-ups and drop-offs for each zone on an hourly basis.
The model must be capable of capturing the complex dynamics of e-scooter demand patterns.
The underlying model of this Demand Forecasting Module is trained using historical demand data extracted from the raw GBFS data (see \autoref{sec:design-data}).
The output of both demand models should be the expected number of pick-up / drop-off events hourly per zone, to be integrated in the proposed rebalancing framework.
To meet these requirements, two separate models are trained: one for predicting pick-ups and another for drop-offs.
This separation is motivated by the observed imbalances between pick-ups and drop-offs, as discussed in \autoref{sec:design-data}.


\subsubsection{Model Selection}
The model selected for the Demand Forecasting Module is the Irregular Convolutional Long Short-Term Memory (IrConvLSTM) network proposed by Li et al. \cite{li_improving_2023}.
The selection of the IrConvLSTM model as part of this rebalancing framework is based on its empirically validated performance, its publicly available open-source implementation, and its suitable architecture for modeling the complex dynamics of micromobility demand data.
Due to the short time scope of this master's thesis, the focus of this work is on the rebalancing framework itself, which is why an open-source implementation for the Demand Forecasting Module is preferred.
Li et al. \cite{li_improving_2023} demonstrated the high performance of the IrConvLSTM model in an empirical study comparing the proposed model against several other forecasting models.
These included traditional time-series models like ARIMA, standalone deep-learning models like LSTM and GRU, and hybrid models like the CNN+LSTM model.
The evaluation was conducted across five different cities using bike-sharing data.
The IrConvLSTM using Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) yielded the best performances, which is why this variant is used for this work.
IrConvLSTM consistently outperformed the other models across all benchmarks, including Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE).\\


\subsubsection{Architecture}
The core of this model is the Irregular Convolution layers, which replace the standard kernels of a Convolutional layer with fixed spatial kernels with semantic kernels based on semantic neighbors.
IrCOnv layers operate on the central cell and its top $N$ semantic neighbours.
By that, the historical demand development of zones with similar demand patterns are incorporated in the prediction for the center zone.
To identify these semantic neighbours, two metrics were proposed: Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC) and Dynamic Time Warping (DTW).
The PCC measures the linear correlation between the historical time series of two cells.
The DTW metric, on the other hand, measures the similarity between two temporal sequences.
\autoref{fig:irconv-neighbors} illustrates these differences between standard kernels used in CNNs and semantic neighbour kernels used in IrConv layers.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{content/figures/IrConv-LSTM-Neighbors.pdf}
    \caption{Comparison of spatial neighbors used in regular convolution and semantic neighbors used in irregular convolution \cite{li_improving_2023}}
    \label{fig:irconv-neighbors}
\end{figure}


The IrConvLSTM consists of three parallel modules, each corresponding to a different scope of historical demand development:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Closeness}:
        The Closeness captures the most recent demand dynamics, enabling the model to react to short-term trends.
        This is done by analyzing an hourly sequence of the demand over the past 24 hours.
    \item \textbf{Period}:
        The Period, on the other hand, concentrates on learning daily commute patterns.
        These patterns are extracted by analyzing demand patterns from the same hour on each of the last seven days.
    \item \textbf{Trend}:
        The Trend reflects the longest time horizon.
        It identifies long-term, weekly patterns that occur in the underlying demand data.
        By analyzing demand data from the same hour over the preceding two weeks, this module integrates long-term patterns in the data.
\end{itemize}
This multi-component structure allows the model to learn from immediate past data, daily periodic patterns, and longer-term weekly trends simultaneously.
Each of these three parallel streams feeds its sequence of historical demand data into a block containing three consecutive Irregular Convolutional (IrConv) layers followed by a flatten layer and an LSTM layer.

\subsubsection{Handling Forecast Uncertainty}
The forecasts generated by this Demand Forecasting Module are biased and will not perfectly reflect the actual demand data.
The RL agent's design accounts for this uncertainty in demand predictions.
The agent does not treat the forecasts as ground truth.
That is why the simulation is driven by the actual demand and not by the forecasts.
Additionally, the rewards for both agents are calculated using the actual realized demand based on the simulation.
This ensures that the agents learn a policy that is effective under real-world conditions, including uncertain conditions such as errors in demand forecasts.
The implementation details of this Demand Forecasting Module and the IrConvLSTM are described in \autoref{sec:implementation_demand-forecasting-module}.
