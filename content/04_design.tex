\chapter{Design}\label{sec:design} % approx. 21 pages
This chapter details the design of the proposed rebalancing framework.
\autoref{sec:design-data} describes the data acquisition and preprocessing, \autoref{sec:design-problem_definition} formally defines the rebalancing problem, and \autoref{sec:design-rl_framework} presents the \gls{hrl} framework.

\section{Data}\label{sec:design-data}
The data underlying this thesis was collected from a \glsfirst{gbfs} \cite{mobilitydata_specification_2024} feed that publishes the live locations of e-scooters from sharing services operating in Baden-Württemberg \cite{mobidata_bw_gebundelte_2022}.
These services utilize a free-floating approach \cite{oeschger_micromobility_2020}, permitting users to pick-up and drop-off vehicles at any location within the operating area, without restriction to specific parking stations.
This section details the collection, preprocessing, and exploratory analysis of the \gls{gbfs} data used to develop this \gls{hrl} framework. 


\subsection{Data Collection}
To capture a comprehensive view of e-scooter usage, data was acquired from the \gls{gbfs} feeds of two of the leading service providers in Stuttgart: Bolt and Voi.
The data was collected by crawling the public feed, creating detailed time-series snapshots of all available vehicles.
These snapshots were stored as JSON files for later use.
The initial data collection interval was set to 15 minutes as a conservative, precautionary measure.
Public data APIs, including the \gls{gbfs} feed used in this work, often employ rate-limiting mechanisms to protect their services from excessive traffic and ensure high availability for all users.
To avoid the risk of the data collection script being automatically flagged or blocked, a longer interval has been choosen to guarantee the establishment of a stable data pipeline.
After confirming the reliability of this process over an initial period, the sampling rate was later refined and reduced to 5 minutes to capture vehicle dynamics with greater temporal resolution.
The subsequent data processing steps address this variation in sampling frequency.
Because the final analysis relies on event data aggregated into one-hour time bins, the differences in the underlying snapshot frequency are normalized.
While the 15-minute interval may theoretically under-capture very short trips compared to the 5-minute data, this effect is considered negligible for the hourly resolution used in the demand analysis and model training.
This process was conducted continuously over a 128-day period, from February 11, 2025, to June 18, 2025.

The \gls{gbfs} format, a standard for publishing shared micromobility vehicle data, provides several key attributes for each vehicle.
These include its unique identifier, GPS coordinates (latitude and longitude), operational status (reserved or disabled), and battery level.
A complete list of the data fields extracted from the feed is presented in \autoref{tab:gbfs_fields}.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}
The primary goal of preprocessing was to transform the raw location snapshots into a dataset of discrete pick-up and drop-off events.
A methodological limitation of the \gls{gbfs} feed is the randomization of vehicle identifiers for user anonymization, which prevents the direct tracking of individual trips.
To overcome this, an inferential approach was adopted: by comparing consecutive snapshots, the disappearance of a vehicle ID from one frame to the next was classified as a pick-up.
In contrast, the appearance of a new ID was classified as a drop-off.
This inference can result in errors, for instance, vehicles taken offline for maintenance may be misidentified as trip pick-ups, and newly deployed vehicles as drop-offs.

This event inference process yielded a total of 2,168,331 combined pick-up and drop-off events for Voi and 680,459 for Bolt.
Subsequently, the dataset was filtered to remove geographical outliers by defining a boundary corresponding to the city of Stuttgart and excluding events in outlying regions (e.g., Böblingen).
This filtering had a notable effect on the Voi dataset, reducing it by 59.6\% to 876,511 events.
In contrast, the Bolt dataset was largely unaffected, retaining 96.4\% of its data with 656,082 events remaining.
\autoref{fig:demand_map_reduction} presents this filtering process by comparing the data displayed on a map before and after filtering.

Finally, the cleaned event data was aggregated to create a structured dataset for model training.
For spatial aggregation, a two-layer hierarchical approach was implemented using the H3 hexagonal grid system \cite{uber_h3_2018}.
The finest spatial unit, a \textit{zone}, was defined using H3 resolution 8, resulting in 291 distinct zones across the operating area.
These zones were then grouped into larger \textit{communities} at H3 resolution 6, producing a total of 9 communities.
This hierarchical structure allows for analysis at both a granular and a regional level.
Temporally, events within each zone were aggregated into one-hour time bins.

This H3 system was chosen for three key reasons.
First, its hierarchical structure facilitates seamless aggregation at different spatial resolutions, which is ideal for the community-zone model used in this thesis.
Second, hexagons provide uniform distances to all six neighbors, ensuring greater consistency in spatial analysis compared to square grids.
Third, as a global standard, H3 ensures that the grid is predefined worldwide, simplifying the potential application of this methodology to other cities.
\autoref{fig:data_grid_community_structure} illustrates the resulting grid structure imposed on the operating area.

\section{Problem Definition}\label{sec:design-problem_definition}
This section formalizes the fleet rebalancing problem of a free-floating e-scooter sharing system.
The problem definition considers both operator-based rebalancing, performed by service workers, and user-based rebalancing, performed by users guided by incentives.
The rebalancing mechanisms addressed are operator-based relocations between communities and user-based relocations within communities, which are achieved through incentives.
The routing problem of service vehicles performing operator-based rebalancing is not addressed in this work, as the focus is on developing a framework for strategic decision-making rather than the operational \gls{vrp}.
The \gls{vrp} for rebalancing is a well-studied field \cite{emami_integrated_2024, lee_battery_2024, jimenez-merono_optimization_2024, paparella_optimization-based_2023, sanchez_simulation_2022}.
It is assumed that an existing \gls{vrp} solver can be used to implement the strategic decisions made by the framework.

The \gls{soc} of the vehicles is also not modeled in the simulation.
This simplification is due to limited data availability, and is a common approach in recent literature \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025, villa-zapata_incorporating_2024, schofield_handling_2021, pan_deep_2019}.
As detailed in \autoref{sec:design-data}, only the trip start and endpoints are available, and no full trip data.
Therefore, modeling whole trips in the simulation, which is necessary to update the \gls{soc} accordingly, isn't directly possible with the available data.
In \autoref{sec:conclusion-future_work}, possible solutions for incorporating the \gls{soc} into the simulation despite these data limitations are discussed.

\subsection{Scope and Notation}\label{sec:design-problem_definition-notation}
The service area is partitioned into a set of zones ($\mathcal{Z}$), which are grouped into communities ($\mathcal{C}$), as displayed in \autoref{fig:data_grid_community_structure}.
The time is discretized into hourly intervals ($\mathcal{T}$) in which all pick-up, drop-off, and rebalancing events occur.
The fleet size ($\mathcal{N}$) remains constant over all timesteps $t$.
\autoref{tab:problem_notation} summarizes all notation used for the problem formulation, including all sets, parameters, and rebalancing outcomes.
The subset of zones for a given community $c$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{Z}_c = \{z \in \mathcal{Z} \mid \kappa(z) = c\}
\end{equation}

\subsection{State Variables}\label{sec:design-problem_definition-states}
The formulated rebalancing problem distinguishes between forecasted demand ($\hat P_z(t)$, $\hat D_z(t)$), which forms part of the state representation used for decision-making, and actual demand ($P_z(t)$, $D_z(t)$), which drives the simulation used for training and evaluation.
All performance metrics, such as the satisfied demand, are computed against the actual demand values.

\subsection{Rebalancing actions}\label{sec:design-problem_definition-actions}
The framework considers both operator-based and user-based rebalancing, which is why the problem definition includes rebalancing outcomes for both approaches.
They do not directly correspond to the action spaces of the RL agents, which are detailed in \autoref{sec:design-rl_framework}.

\subsubsection{Operator-based rebalancing}
Operator-based rebalancing is performed to relocate e-scooters between communities.
Service workers move e-scooters between communities, which is represented by $X_{c\rightarrow c'}(t)$, describing the number of vehicles relocated from community $c$ to community $c'$ at timestep $t$.
The rebalancing outcomes on the community level are mapped to the zone level using the $\Delta_{op}(t)_z$ function, which is described in more detail in \autoref{sec:design-problem_definition-dynamics}.

\subsubsection{User-based rebalancing}
User-based rebalancing is performed between zones inside a community.
Users are incentivized to drop-off vehicles at a different zone than they originally intended to.
The rebalancing outcome $Y_{z\rightarrow z'}(t)$ describes such a relocation done by a user from zone $z$ to $z'$.
\autoref{eq:rb-user-constraint-spatial} constrains user-based rebalancing to occur only within a community.
Additionally, user-based rebalancing operations are restricted to the neighboring zones.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rb-user-constraint-spatial}
Y_{z\to z'}(t) \;=\; 0
\quad
\forall\,t\in\mathcal{T},\;\forall\,z,z'\in\mathcal{Z}
\text{ such that }
\bigl(A_{z,z'}=0\bigr)\;\lor\;\bigl(\kappa(z)\neq\kappa(z')\bigr).
\end{equation}

\subsection{System Dynamics}\label{sec:design-problem_definition-dynamics}
 
\autoref{eq:rp-fleet-conservation} formulates the global fleet conservation.
This constraint ensures that the total number of e-scooters across all zones remains constant at every time period $t$.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:rp-fleet-conservation}
    \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}}n_z(t) = \mathcal{N}, \forall t\in \mathcal{T}
\end{equation}


\autoref{eq:pb-demand-flow} illustrates the demand flow of a single zone, defining the number of e-scooters at time $t+1$ by accounting for demand and relocation operations.
This demand flow enables a step-wise calculation of available scooters at every timestep $t$.
The demand flow considers both pick-up demand $P_z(t)$ and drop-off demand $D_z(t)$.
Additionally, rebalancing operations are considered by the rebalancing outcomes $X_{c\rightarrow c'}(t)$ and $Y_{z\rightarrow z'}(t)$.
Operator-based rebalancing is incorporated through the $\Delta_{op}(t)_z$ function, which represents the net change in vehicles for zone $z$.
This function takes the set of all community-level rebalancing outcomes, $X_{c\rightarrow c'}(t)$, and the current vehicle counts, ${n}(t)$, as its parameters.
Based on these inputs, it implements a priority-based heuristic, where incoming vehicles are allocated to the zones with the lowest e-scooter counts.
Accordingly, outgoing vehicles are taken from the zones with the highest counts.
This simplified assumption is made within the defined scope of how operator-based rebalancing should be utilized in this framework.
Operator-based rebalancing should focus on strategic rebalancing between communities, and therefore does not specify fine-grained zone-level drop-off locations.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:pb-demand-flow}
    \begin{aligned}
        n_z(t+1) =\;&\ n_z(t) - P_z(t) + D_z(t) \\
        &+ \Delta_{op}(t)_z \\
        &+ \sum_{z' \in \mathcal{Z}} Y_{z'\rightarrow z}(t) - \sum_{z' \in \mathcal{Z}} Y_{z\rightarrow z'}(t)
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Objective}\label{sec:design-problem_definition-objective}

\autoref{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand} defines the satisfied demand, which is the demand that the service could satisfy with enough vehicles.
This satisfied demand is used to calculate the \glsfirst{sdr}, which defines the percentage of the requested demand to the amount of demand that could be satisfied by the system (see \autoref{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand-ratio}).

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand}
        S_z(t) = \min\{P_z(t), n_z(t) + D_z(t)\}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:problem-definition-satisfied-demand-ratio}
        r^S_z(t) = 
        \begin{cases}
            \frac{S_z(t)}{P_z(t)}, & P_z(t) > 0,\\[8pt]
            0, & P_z(t) = 0.
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
\end{subequations}

To quantify the spatial imbalance of the fleet, the Gini coefficient is utilized, a metric used to promote fairness in rebalancing systems \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025}.
A Gini coefficient of 0 represents a perfectly uniform distribution, with an equal number of scooters in every zone.
A value of 1 indicates maximum imbalance, where all vehicles are concentrated in one zone.
Minimizing this value helps prevent service dead zones and improves overall accessibility.
This imbalance is measured at two scales: globally across the entire service area, and locally within each community.
The Gini coefficient for the entire service area, $G(t)$, is defined as:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:pb-objective-gini}
G(t) \;=\; 
\frac{1}{2 \, |\mathcal{Z}|^2 \, \bar{n}(t)} 
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{Z}} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{Z}} 
\bigl\lvert n_i(t) - n_j(t)\bigr\rvert,
\qquad
\bar{n}(t) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Z}|} \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}} n_z(t).
\end{equation}

Similarly, the Gini coefficient for a single community $c$, used to measure intra-community imbalance, is defined as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pb-objective-gini-community}
G_c(t) \;=\; 
\frac{1}{2 \, |\mathcal{Z}_c|^2 \, \bar{n}_c(t)} 
\sum_{i \in \mathcal{Z}_c} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{Z}_c} 
\bigl\lvert n_i(t) - n_j(t)\bigr\rvert,
\qquad
\bar{n}_c(t) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Z}_c|} \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}_c} n_z(t).
\end{equation}

\autoref{eq:pb-objective} defines the objective of the rebalancing problem.
Its primary goal is to maximize the SDR while reducing the costs associated with operator- and user-based rebalancing.
Additionally, an imbalance penalty based on the Gini coefficient is included to promote service equity.
The objective is calculated over the whole simulation horizon, e.g., over all time steps $t$.
This equation serves as a high-level, conceptual formulation of the overall system's goals.
It is intentionally presented as a simplified, unweighted sum to provide a clear and concise problem statement.
This structure acts as an abstraction of the more detailed, operational reward functions that guide the individual \gls{rl} agents, which are described in the following sections.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:pb-objective}
\max\;\sum_{t\in \mathcal{T}}\Biggl[
  \sum_{z\in \mathcal{Z}} r^S_z(t)
  \;+\;(1 - \frac{\sum_{c,c' \in \mathcal{C}} X_{c\to c'}(t)}{\mathcal{N}})
  \;+\;(1 - \frac{\sum_{z,z' \in \mathcal{Z}} Y_{z\to z'}(t)}{\mathcal{N}})
  \;+\;(1 - G(t))
\Biggr].
\end{equation}


This problem can be modeled using two coupled \glspl{mdp}, where the rebalancing outcomes $X_{c\rightarrow c'}(t)$ and $Y_{z\rightarrow z'}(t)$ result from the actions taken within a two-tier HRL framework.
This global objective function defines the overall optimization goal.
In the following section, the \gls{hrl} framework is described, which is designed to maximize this objective function.
Additionally, this section details how this global objective is decomposed into specific reward functions tailored to particular agents.

\section{Framework}\label{sec:design-rl_framework}
A \glsfirst{hrl} framework is proposed to address the e-scooter rebalancing problem.
The \gls{hrl} framework comprises two tiers.
The first tier, the \glsfirst{rdc}, manages inter-community imbalances by determining the target number of vehicles for each defined community.
These imbalances are addressed through operator-based rebalancing, where service workers collect vehicles from communities with an oversupply and redistribute them to communities experiencing an undersupply.
The second tier consists of multiple \glsentryfullpl{uic}, each assigned to a specific community, to manage intra-community imbalances, i.e., imbalances within a single community.
These coordinators aim to promote an equal distribution of vehicles by offering incentives to users for returning vehicles to designated undersupplied zones at the end of their trips.
\autoref{fig:hrl-architecture} illustrates the architecture of this two-tier \gls{hrl} framework.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.9\linewidth]{content/figures/hrl_architecture.pdf}
    \caption{The Two-Tier \glsfirst{hrl} Framework. This diagram illustrates the conceptual architecture of the proposed \gls{hrl} framework, which decomposes the rebalancing problem into two distinct levels. Tier 1 consists of a single, high-level \glsfirst{rdc} who performs strategic, operator-based rebalancing between communities. Tier 2 consists of multiple low-level \glsentryfullpl{uic}, each responsible for tactical, user-based rebalancing within a single community. This hierarchical decomposition allows the framework to address both large-scale and local imbalances simultaneously.}

The hierarchical approach has been chosen due to its ability to decompose a large problem, such as the rebalancing problem of an e-scooter sharing service, into more manageable sub-problems \cite{pateria_hierarchical_2021}.
In this \gls{hrl} framework, the rebalancing problem is decomposed into sub-problems representing different spatial and operational levels.
The \gls{rdc} addresses the rebalancing problem on a macroscopic community level, while the \gls{uic} handles it on a microscopic zone level.

A dedicated demand forecasting module supports both the \gls{rdc} and the \glspl{uic}.
This module provides demand predictions as part of the state for both tiers, enhancing the effectiveness of rebalancing operations by allowing the system to anticipate future demand.
This modular design ensures that both tiers optimize rebalancing decisions based on consistent future outlooks and separates the demand forecasting task from the \gls{rl} agents.

\subsection{Regional Distribution Coordinator (RDC)}\label{sec:design-rl_framework-rdc}
The \glsfirst{rdc} constitutes the first tier of the proposed two-tier \gls{hrl} framework.
Its primary purpose is to address inter-community imbalances by determining the target number of e-scooters for each defined community through operator-based rebalancing.

\subsubsection{Markov Decision Process (MDP) formulation}
The \gls{rdc} is modeled as an \gls{mdp}, defined by a tuple $(\mathcal{S, A, P, R})$, where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space, $\mathcal{A}$ is the action space, $\mathcal{P}$ is the state transition probability function, and $\mathcal{R}$ is the reward function \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.
Due to the complex dynamics of shared e-scooter systems, the transition probability function $\mathcal{P}$ is learned implicitly by the agent through interaction with the simulation environment.
The \gls{mdp} is assumed to be stationary, meaning that the transition probabilities depend only on the current state and the selected action and not on the specific timestep $t$.
This \gls{mdp} formulation is illustrated in \autoref{fig:rdc_mdp}.

\paragraph{State ($\mathcal{S}^{RDC}_t$)}
The state representation for the \gls{rdc} at timestep $t$, denoted $\mathcal{S}^{RDC}_t$, is a triple containing community-level vehicle counts ($n_c(t)$), pick-up demand forecast ($\hat{P}_c(t)$), and drop-off demand forecast ($\hat{D}_c(t)$).
These demand forecasts are generated by a separate demand forecasting module, detailed in \autoref{sec:design-rl_framework-forecasting_module}.


\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Community-level Vehicle Counts ($n_c(t)$):}
    The current number of available e-scooters in the community $c$ at timestep $t$.
    \item \textbf{Pick-up Demand Forecast ($\hat{P}_c(t)$):}
    The forecasted number of e-scooter trips expected to originate from community $c$ during the next time interval ($t \to t+1$).
    \item \textbf{Drop-off Demand Forecast ($\hat{D}_c(t)$):}
    The forecasted number of e-scooter trips expected to end in community $c$ during the next time interval ($t \to t+1$).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Action ($\mathcal{A}^{RDC}_t$)}
The \gls{rdc}'s action at timestep $t$, $\mathcal{A}^{RDC}_t$, is a vector $[a_1(t), a_2(t), ..., a_{N_{\mathcal{C}}}(t)]$, where $N_{\mathcal{C}}$ is the total number of communities.
Each component $a_c(t)$ specifies the rebalancing quota for community $c$, representing the target number of e-scooters to be added to (positive value) or removed from (negative value) that community.
The action space for each component $a_c(t)$ is discrete and identical across communities. For this study, the action space for each $a_c(t)$ is defined as $\{-8, -4, -2, 0, 2, 4, 8\}$, providing seven distinct rebalancing options per community.

The \gls{rdc} aims to select actions that balance supply across all communities.
Although ideal actions may intrinsically maintain the total fleet size, the \gls{rdc} can select a set of actions that, when aggregated, would result in a change in the total number of vehicles.
The rebalancing algorithm, which is part of the simulation environment, is responsible for managing such scenarios.
If necessary, this algorithm scales the raw action values (quotas) proportionally to ensure that the total fleet size remains constant, thereby preserving the \gls{rdc}'s intended rebalancing direction while adhering to operational constraints.

\paragraph{Reward ($\mathcal{R}^{RDC}_t$)}
The \gls{rdc}'s performance is quantified by a reward function designed to guide the agent towards effective rebalancing strategies.
It is calculated after the execution of the rebalancing action $\mathcal{A}^{RDC}_t$ and the simulation of user demand flow for the current timestep. The total reward $\mathcal{R}^{RDC}_t$ is a weighted sum of three components.
These components reflect the primary operational goals: maximizing service utility, minimizing operational costs, and ensuring an equitable vehicle distribution.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:rdc-reward}
    \mathcal{R}^{RDC}_t = \frac{w_{demand}^{RDC} \cdot R_{demand}^{RDC}(t) + w_{cost}^{RDC} \cdot R_{cost}^{RDC}(t) + w_{gini}^{RDC} \cdot R_{gini}^{RDC}(t)}{w_{demand}^{RDC}+ w_{cost}^{RDC}+ w_{gini}^{RDC}}
\end{equation}
where $w_{demand}^{RDC}$, $w_{cost}^{RDC}$, and $w_{gini}^{RDC}$ are non-negative weighting factors. Each component reward ($R_{demand}^{RDC}(t)$, $R_{cost}^{RDC}(t)$, $R_{gini}^{RDC}(t)$) is individually normalized to a range of [0, 1].
The specific values for the weighting factors and the methodology for their selection are detailed in \autoref{sec:implementation_rdc}.

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rdc-reward-demand}
        R_{demand}^{RDC}(t) = \frac{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}}S_z(t)}{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}}D_z(t)}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rdc-reward-cost}
        R_{cost}^{RDC}(t) = 1 -  \left(\frac{\sum_{c, c' \in \mathcal{C}}X_{c \to c'}(t)}{\mathcal{N}}\right)^2
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rdc-reward-gini}
        R_{gini}^{RDC}(t) = 1 - G(t)
    \end{equation}
\end{subequations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Satisfied Demand Ratio ($R_{demand}^{RDC}(t)$):}
    This core component quantifies the effectiveness of rebalancing actions by measuring the proportion of satisfied trip requests.
    It is calculated by summing, over all zones in the entire system, the number of trip requests the system successfully served, and dividing this sum by the total number of trip requests received during the current time interval (see \autoref{eq:rdc-reward-demand}).
    This ratio results in a stable reward signal between 0 and 1.
    \item \textbf{Rebalancing Cost ($R_{cost}^{RDC}(t)$):}
    To penalize excessive rebalancing, this component incorporates the cost of rebalancing operations.
    It is calculated by summing the total number of vehicles rebalanced (picked up or dropped off by operators) in the current timestep.
    This sum is then divided by the total fleet size to receive a normalized rebalancing ratio.
    This ratio is squared and then transformed to ensure that a higher number of rebalancing operations results in a lower value for this reward component, and vice versa (see \autoref{eq:rdc-reward-cost}).
    \item \textbf{Imbalance Penalty ($R_{gini}^{RDC}(t)$):}
    This component encourages an equitable distribution of the e-scooter fleet across communities.
    It is derived from the Gini coefficient, a measure of statistical dispersion, calculated over the vehicle counts in all communities.
    The Gini coefficient itself ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality).
    This value is then transformed to align with the reward structure (see \autoref{eq:rdc-reward-gini}), resulting in a normalized component that is high when the e-scooter fleet is evenly distributed and low for an imbalanced distribution \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Algorithm and Architecture}
Given this \gls{mdp} formulation, a \gls{mh-dqn} is employed for the Regional Distribution Coordinator. \gls{mh-dqn} is a value-based reinforcement learning algorithm that utilizes deep neural networks to approximate the optimal action-value function, $Q*(s, a)$ \cite{mnih_human-level_2015}.
The multi-head variant of the standard \gls{dqn} \cite{li_personalized_2024} is particularly suitable for the \gls{rdc} as it needs to output multiple, correlated rebalancing actions, one for each community, simultaneously.
Alternative architectures, such as a single network predicting a composite action vector or Q-values for all joint actions, were considered but deemed impractical due to the combinatorial explosion of the action space.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{content/figures/mh-dqn-architecture.pdf}
    \caption{The \glsfirst{mh-dqn} Architecture for the \gls{rdc}. This diagram illustrates the neural network architecture for the \gls{rdc} agent. A normalized input state is first processed by a series of shared layers that learn a common feature representation for the entire system. This shared representation is then fed into multiple independent \textit{heads,} where each \textit{head} is a smaller neural network that outputs the Q-values for the discrete rebalancing actions of a single community. This multi-head design allows the agent to efficiently learn correlated but distinct policies for each community from a unified system state.}
    \label{fig:mdh-architecture}
\end{figure}

The core architecture of the \gls{mh-dqn} comprises three main types of layers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Input Layer:}
    Processes the state representation ($\mathcal{S}^{RDC}_t$), involving flattening and normalization of the input features (community-level vehicle counts and demand forecasts).
    \item \textbf{Shared Layers:}
    These layers receive input from the Input Layer and learn to extract features representing system-wide metrics and relationships relevant to all communities.
    \item \textbf{Head-Specific Layers:}
    A separate set of neural network layers, referred to as a \textit{head}, is dedicated to each community.
    Each \textit{head} takes the processed features from the Shared Layers and outputs the Q-values for each discrete rebalancing action $a_c(t)$ applicable to its corresponding community.
    The action selected for a community is the one with the highest Q-value from its respective \textit{head}.
\end{enumerate}
Each \textit{head} thus determines the optimal rebalancing quota for its assigned community based on the global system state and learned rebalancing policies.

\paragraph{Prioritized Experience Replay (PER)}
The \gls{mh-dqn} utilizes \gls{per} \cite{schaul_prioritized_2016} to enhance learning efficiency.
Standard \gls{dqn} stores transitions $(s_t, a_t, r_t, s_{t+1})$ in a replay buffer and samples them uniformly for training.
With \gls{per}, transitions are sampled non-uniformly based on their significance, measured by the magnitude of the \gls{td-error}.
Prioritizing transitions with high \gls{td-error}, which are transitions where the agent learns the most, accelerates the learning process and improves sample efficiency.
However, this prioritization introduces a bias by modifying the distribution of training data.

This bias is counterbalanced by \gls{is} weights, which adjust the contribution of each sampled transition during the learning updates.
These \gls{is}-weights are controlled by an exponent $\beta_{PER}$, which is annealed from an initial value towards one over the course of training (see \autoref{sec:implementation_rdc} for the exact annealing schedule).
This annealing schedule for $\beta_{PER}$ helps to balance the benefits of prioritized sampling with the need for unbiased updates, contributing to stable and efficient learning \cite{schaul_prioritized_2016}.

\paragraph{Exploration Strategy}
To ensure sufficient exploration of the state-action space, the \gls{rdc} employs an $\epsilon$-greedy strategy.
In $\epsilon$-greedy, $\epsilon$ represents the probability of choosing a random action rather than the action considered optimal by the current policy.
To encourage broad exploration initially and gradually shift towards exploitation, $\epsilon$ is annealed over the course of the training process (see \autoref{sec:implementation_rdc} for the exact $\epsilon$ schedule).
It starts at a high value at the beginning of training and is reduced to a small, non-zero value by the end of training.
Maintaining a small $\epsilon$ ensures persistent exploration, which can be beneficial in complex and dynamic environments \cite{zhang_why_2020}.

\paragraph{Training stabilization techniques}
Several techniques are incorporated to stabilize the training of the \gls{mh-dqn}.
To address the bias in Q-learning, where Q-values are frequently overestimated, a \gls{ddqn} algorithm \cite{hasselt_deep_2016} is integrated.
\gls{ddqn} decouples action selection from action evaluation for estimating the value of the next state.
Specifically, the policy network determines the optimal action for the next state, and the target network then evaluates the Q-value of this selected action.

A separate target network, with an identical architecture to the policy network (the network being actively trained), is used to provide stable targets for updating the Q-value.
The target network's weights are not updated directly through backpropagation.
Instead, they are periodically updated using soft updates, where its weights ($\theta_{target}$) become a weighted average of its own current weights and the policy network's weights ($\theta_{policy}$): $\theta_{target} \leftarrow \tau \theta_{policy} + (1-\tau)\theta_{target}$, for a small $\tau$ (see \autoref{sec:implementation_rdc} for hyperparameter values).
This combination of a separate target network and soft updates helps stabilize the training process.

Furthermore, to enhance performance and maintain training stability, a learning rate schedule is employed.
The learning rate starts higher to accelerate initial learning and is gradually decreased to facilitate fine-tuning and convergence to more optimal solutions.
Additionally, gradient clipping is applied during backpropagation to prevent exploding gradients, a common issue that can destabilize the training of deep neural networks \cite{zhang_why_2020}.

\subsection{User-Incentive Coordinator (UIC)}\label{sec:design-rl_framework-uic}
The second tier of the proposed \gls{hrl} framework comprises multiple \glsentryfullpl{uic}, each assigned to a specific community to manage intra-community imbalances.
Intra-community imbalances are addressed through user-based rebalancing.
The \gls{uic} agents propose incentives for each zone that is part of the designated community to encourage users to drop-off vehicles at the end of their journey at zones that experience an undersupply of vehicles.


\subsubsection{Markov Decision Process (MDP) formulation}
The \gls{uic}'s sequential decision-making problem is formalized as an \gls{mdp}.
A diagram of this formulation is provided in \autoref{fig:uic_mdp}.

\paragraph{State ($\mathcal{S}^{UIC}_t$)}
The state representation for the \gls{uic} at timestep $t$, denoted as $\mathcal{S}^{UIC}_t$, is a triple containing zone level vehicle counts ($n_z(t)$), pick-up demand forecast ($\hat{P}_z(t)$), and drop-off demand forecast ($\hat{D}_z(t)$).
The state representation of the \gls{uic} is similarly defined as the state space of the \gls{rdc}, but scoped to the specific community to which the \gls{uic} is designated.
The state representation consists of three components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Zone-level Vehicle Counts ($n_z(t)$):}
    The current number of available e-scooters in the zone $z$ at the current timestep $t$. Where the community $\kappa(z)$ is the designated community of the \gls{uic}.
    \item \textbf{Pick-up Demand Forecast ($\hat{P}_z(t)$):}
    The forecasted number of e-scooters that are expected to originate at zone $z$ during the next time interval ($t \to t+1$). Where the community $\kappa(z)$ is the designated community of the \gls{uic}.
    \item \textbf{Drop-off demand forecast ($\hat{D}_z(t)$):}
    The forecasted number of e-scooters that are expected to be dropped off by users, e.g., the number of trips that are ending at zone $z$ in the next time interval ($t \to t+1$). Where the community $\kappa(z)$ is the designated community of the \gls{uic}.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Action ($\mathcal{A}^{UIC}_t$)}
The \gls{uic}'s action space is continuous.
The \gls{uic}'s actions represent continuous incentive levels between $[0,1]$ for each zone $z$ that is part of the designated community $c$.
This results in the following definition of the Actions:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A}^{UIC}_{t, c} = [a_1^{UIC}(t), ..., a_{N_{\mathcal{Z}_c}}^{UIC}(t)]
\end{equation}

$N_{\mathcal{Z}_c}$ defines the number of zones in community $c$.



\paragraph{Reward ($\mathcal{R}^{UIC}_t$)}

The reward function $\mathcal{R}^{UIC}_t$ of the \gls{uic} considers three components, each weighted by a non-negative weighting factor.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:uic-reward}
    \mathcal{R}^{UIC}_t(c) = \frac{w_{demand}^{UIC} \cdot R_{demand}^{UIC}(t, c) + w_{cost}^{UIC} \cdot R_{cost}^{UIC}(t, c) + w_{gini}^{UIC} \cdot R_{gini}^{UIC}(t, c)}{w_{demand}^{UIC}+ w_{cost}^{UIC}+ w_{gini}^{UIC}}
\end{equation}
where $w_{demand}^{UIC}$, $w_{cost}^{UIC}$, and $w_{gini}^{UIC}$ are non-negative weighting factors. Each component reward ($R_{demand}^{UIC}(t)$, $R_{cost}^{UIC}(t)$, $R_{gini}^{UIC}(t)$) is individually normalized to a range of [0, 1].
The specific values for the weighting factors and the methodology for their selection are detailed in \autoref{sec:implementation_uic}.

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:uic-reward-demand}
        R_{demand}^{UIC}(t, c) = \frac{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}_c}S_z(t)}{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}_c}D_z(t)}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:uic-reward-cost}
        R_{cost}^{UIC}(t, c) = 1 -  \left(\frac{\sum_{z, z' \in \mathcal{Z}_c}Y_{z \to z'}(t)}{\sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}_c}n_z(t)}\right)^2
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:uic-reward-gini}
        R_{gini}^{UIC}(t, c) = 1 - G_c(t)
    \end{equation}
\end{subequations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Satisfied Demand Ratio ($R_{demand}^{UIC}(t, c)$):}
    The core component of the reward function is the \glsfirst{sdr}, which measures the proportion of trip requests that are satisfied.
    It is calculated by summing the number of trip requests originating from the designated community the system successfully served, and dividing this sum by the total number of trip requests received for each zone (see \autoref{eq:uic-reward-demand}).
    This \gls{sdr} is calculated for the time interval $[t, t+1]$. 
    
    \item \textbf{Rebalancing Cost ($R_{cost}^{UIC}(t, c)$):}
    Rebalancing operations negatively change the reward signal to encourage the \gls{uic} to limit rebalancing operations to only necessary operations.
    This rebalancing cost component is calculated by summing the number of vehicles rebalanced through user incentives inside the corresponding community, which is divided by the current number of vehicles present in this community.
    This results in a normalized ratio of possible rebalancing operations.
    This ratio is squared and inverted to penalize high numbers of rebalancing operations (see \autoref{eq:uic-reward-cost}).
    A low number of rebalancing operations results in higher values for this reward component.
    
    \item \textbf{Imbalance Penalty ($R_{gini}^{UIC}(t, c)$):}
    The last component of the reward signal of the \gls{uic} encourages an equitable distribution of the e-scooter fleet inside the community \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025}.
    It is calculated based on the Gini coefficient evaluated over the vehicle counts in all zones part of the designated community.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{User Incentive Mechanism}
A critical aspect of the \gls{uic}'s design is the mechanism by which its actions and incentive levels translate into environmental outcomes.
This subsection formalizes the assumed user response model, which defines how users react to incentives.
The \gls{uic} agent interacts with a simulated environment of the e-scooter sharing system.
This models the complex system dynamics of such sharing systems in a scoped and simplified manner for training performance reasons.

Inputs to the \gls{uic}'s environment contain the forecasted pick-up and drop-off demands for all zones inside the community.
These forecasted demands come from the Demand Forecasting Module, which is detailed in \autoref{sec:design-rl_framework-forecasting_module}.
The current vehicle counts per zone are part of the environment system state.

Output from the \gls{uic} agent is a vector of continuous values for each zone in the range [0.0, 1.0] representing the incentive intensity for each zone part of the designated community.
These normalized incentive intensities are handled by a user response model part of the environment.
This model is defined by a user willingness function (\texttt{user\_willingness\_fn}).
This function maps incentive intensity values to a user's willingness factor.
This factor represents the fraction of users who accept the offered incentive.
In the simulation, the neighboring zones of the anticipated drop-off zone are identified, and the corresponding incentive intensities are derived from the selected action from the \gls{uic}.
From these neighboring zones, the zone with the highest incentive value is picked, and the user's willingness is calculated to update the drop-off demand for the current timestep.
This model represents a rational user choice based purely on the highest local incentive offered.
This \texttt{user\_willingness\_fn} is a simplifying assumption that models complex human behaviour.
This simplification is necessary for the simulation environment.

\subsubsection{Algorithm and Architecture}
A \glsfirst{ppo} \cite{schulman_proximal_2017} algorithm is implemented for the User-Incentive Coordinators.
PPO was selected as it can effectively handle the continuous action spaces required for a fine-grained setting of incentive intensities.
Additionally, PPO is renowned for its training stability and sample efficiency \cite{schulman_proximal_2017}.
This makes it a more practical and reliable choice than more sensitive off-policy algorithms, particularly when training multiple agents in a complex and dynamic environment \cite{schulman_high-dimensional_2018}.

This algorithm, an on-policy actor-critic method as discussed in \autoref{sec:background-reinforcement_learning-pgm}, allocates incentives to specific zones within a community to encourage users to drop-off vehicles in these targeted zones.
The implementation leverages the \gls{sb3} library, which provides a robust and stable implementation of the \gls{ppo} algorithm~\cite{raffin_stable-baselines3_2021}.

The implementation uses a \glsfirst{mlp} architecture with separate networks for the policy (\textit{actor}) and value (\textit{critic}) functions.
Utilizing separate networks can improve stability by preventing the learning objectives of the policy and value functions from interfering with each other's gradients.
By isolating the \textit{actor} and the \textit{critic} into two distinct models, each network is updated with its own loss, thereby eliminating inferences between the two loss signals.
Both networks consist of three hidden layers, using the same network parameters, which are described in \autoref{sec:implementation_uic}.
For each zone in its community, the \textit{actor} network outputs the mean and standard deviation parameters of a Gaussian distribution.
The continuous incentive for each zone is then sampled from the resulting distribution.
This sampling from a Gaussian distribution naturally results in exploration.
The \textit{critic} network, on the other hand, is used for calculating the advantage of each action.
Therefore, the \textit{critic} network outputs a single scalar value representing the estimate of the state-value function.
This estimate serves as a baseline to compute the advantage for each action.
The architecture is illustrated in \autoref{fig:ppo-architecture} \cite{raffin_stable-baselines3_2021}.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{content/figures/ppo-architecture.pdf}
    \caption{\glsfirst{ppo} Architecture for the \gls{uic} Agent. This diagram illustrates the actor-critic neural network architecture employed by the \gls{uic} agents. The architecture consists of two separate \glsentryfullpl{mlp}: an \textit{Actor} (Policy) network and a \textit{Critic} (Value) network. The \textit{Actor} network outputs the parameters (mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$) of a Gaussian probability distribution from which a continuous incentive action is sampled. The \textit{Critic} network outputs a single scalar estimate of the state-value, $V(s)$, which is used to guide the policy updates.}

\end{figure}

\paragraph{Exploration Strategy}
In contrast to the $\epsilon$-greedy strategy used for the \glspl{rdc} \gls{mh-dqn} agent, the \gls{ppo} algorithm with a continuous action space features a stochastic nature, thereby ensuring the exploration of the search space.
The degree of exploration is controlled by an entropy bonus, weighted by a coefficient.
This entropy bonus is added to the loss function to encourage the policy to maintain a higher randomness, preventing the model from converging to a suboptimal solution \cite{schulman_proximal_2017}.

\paragraph{Training stabilization techniques}
\gls{ppo} employs a Clipped Surrogate Objective function that ensures stable policy updates \cite{schulman_proximal_2017}.
This mechanism prevents large policy updates that could potentially cause instability.
The constraint is controlled by a clip range parameter, which is annealed over the training process.
Therefore, larger exploratory updates are allowed at the beginning of the training process.
Gradually decreasing the value of this parameter results in more granular updates at the end of the process.

Additionally, to further stabilize training, \gls{gae} is used \cite{schulman_high-dimensional_2018}.
\gls{gae} computes a weighted average of the advantage estimates over multiple timesteps, rather than relying on a single-step advantage value.
This technique, therefore, produces more stable estimates of the advantage function, resulting in faster learning and more reliable policy gradients. 

\subsection{Demand Forecasting Module}\label{sec:design-rl_framework-forecasting_module}
The successful operation of the rebalancing algorithm is fundamentally dependent on its ability to anticipate future demand to proactively rebalance vehicles based on future demand.
Therefore, a dedicated Demand Forecasting Module that accurately predicts future demand is critical.
By predicting future pick-up and drop-off events, this module enables the \gls{rl} agents to pre-position vehicles to meet anticipated demand.
This results in a proactive rebalancing strategy that can respond actively to changes in demand.
This implementation of an independent Demand Forecasting Module removes the forecasting problem from the rebalancing algorithms themselves, which ideally should increase the overall efficiency of the framework, as the problems are separated and can be targeted directly.

To effectively support the rebalancing agents mentioned earlier, the Demand Forecasting Module must predict the number of pick-ups and drop-offs for each zone on an hourly basis.
The model must be capable of capturing the complex dynamics of e-scooter demand patterns.
The underlying model of this Demand Forecasting Module is trained using historical demand data extracted from the raw \gls{gbfs} data (see \autoref{sec:design-data}).
The output of the demand model should be the expected number of pick-up / drop-off events hourly per zone, to be integrated in the proposed rebalancing framework.
To meet these requirements, two separate models are trained: one for predicting pick-ups and another for drop-offs.
This separation is motivated by the observed imbalances between pick-ups and drop-offs, as discussed in \autoref{sec:design-data}.


\subsubsection{Model Selection}
The model selected for the Demand Forecasting Module is the \glsfirst{irconvlstm} network proposed by Li et al. \cite{li_improving_2023}.
The selection of the \gls{irconvlstm} model as part of this rebalancing framework is based on its empirically validated performance, its publicly available open-source implementation, and its suitable architecture for modeling the complex dynamics of micromobility demand data.
Due to the short time scope of this master's thesis, the focus of this work is on the rebalancing framework itself, which is why an open-source implementation for the Demand Forecasting Module is preferred.
Li et al. \cite{li_improving_2023} demonstrated the high performance of the \gls{irconvlstm} model in an empirical study comparing the proposed model against several other forecasting models.
These included traditional time-series models like ARIMA, standalone deep-learning models like \gls{lstm} and \gls{gru}, and hybrid models like the CNN+LSTM model.
The evaluation was conducted across five different cities using bike-sharing data.
The \gls{irconvlstm} yielded the best performance, which is why this variant is used for this work.
\gls{irconvlstm} consistently outperformed the other models across all benchmarks.\\


\subsubsection{Architecture}
The core of this model are the \glsfirst{irconv} layers, which replace the standard kernels of a Convolutional layer with fixed spatial kernels with semantic kernels based on semantic neighbors.
\gls{irconv} layers operate on the central cell and its top $N$ semantic neighbours.
This allows the prediction to incorporate the historical demand development of zones with similar demand patterns.
To identify these semantic neighbours, two metrics were proposed: \gls{pcc} and \gls{dtw}.
The \gls{pcc} measures the linear correlation between the historical time series of two cells.
The \gls{dtw} metric \cite{berndt_using_1994}, on the other hand, measures the similarity between two temporal sequences.
\autoref{fig:irconv-neighbors} illustrates these differences between standard kernels used in \glspl{cnn} and semantic neighbour kernels used in IrConv layers.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{content/figures/IrConv-LSTM-Neighbors.pdf}

The \gls{irconvlstm} consists of three parallel modules, each corresponding to a different scope of historical demand development:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Closeness}:
        The Closeness captures the most recent demand dynamics, enabling the model to react to short-term trends.
        This is done by analyzing an hourly sequence of the demand over the past 24 hours.
    \item \textbf{Period}:
        The Period, on the other hand, concentrates on learning daily commute patterns.
        These patterns are extracted by analyzing demand patterns from the same hour on each of the last seven days.
    \item \textbf{Trend}:
        The Trend reflects the longest time horizon.
        It identifies long-term, two-week patterns that occur in the underlying demand data.
        By analyzing demand data from the same hour over the preceding two weeks, this module integrates long-term patterns in the data.
\end{itemize}
This multi-component structure allows the model to learn from immediate past data, daily periodic patterns, and longer-term two-week trends simultaneously.
Each of these three parallel streams feeds its sequence of historical demand data into a block containing three consecutive \gls{irconv} layers followed by a flatten layer and an \gls{lstm} layer.

\subsubsection{Handling Forecast Uncertainty}
The forecasts generated by this Demand Forecasting Module are biased and will not perfectly reflect the actual demand data.
The \gls{rl} agent's design accounts for this uncertainty in demand predictions.
The agent does not treat the forecasts as ground truth.
That is why the simulation is driven by the actual demand and not by the forecasts.
Additionally, the rewards for both agents are calculated using the actual realized demand based on the simulation.
This ensures that the agents learn a policy that is effective under real-world conditions, including uncertain conditions such as errors in demand forecasts.
The implementational details of this Demand Forecasting Module and the \gls{irconvlstm} are described in \autoref{sec:implementation_demand-forecasting-module}.
