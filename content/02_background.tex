\section{Micromobility Sharing Services}\label{sec:background-mss}
Shared micromobility refers to a service that allows users to rent a vehicle for short trips within the service provider's operating area.
Typically, the vehicles are lightweight electric vehicles such as electric scooters or electric bikes.
Micromobility sharing services offer customers a flexible and environmentally friendly alternative for individual mobility.
Electric scooters (e-scooters) or electric bikes (e-bikes) are the preferred vehicles of micromobility sharing services, due to their compactness, motor-assistance, and sufficient speed \cite{liao_electric_2022}.


E-scooters and e-bikes both offer many benefits.
E-scooters typically serve shorter-distance journeys, averaging approximately 1.8 kilometers per trip, whereas e-bikes are generally preferred for slightly longer distances, averaging about 2 kilometers.
Trips of e-scooters and e-bikes not only differ in length but also in their purpose.
E-scooter trips are often for leisure, such as shopping or social events.
The second most common use of e-scooters is commuting trips, which is the dominant use of shared e-bikes \cite{liao_electric_2022}.

In urban areas, e-scooters and e-bikes have been identified as promising modes for reducing carbon emissions and air pollutants.
Electrically powered vehicles have the potential to achieve carbon neutrality when powered by renewable energy sources.
Recent studies in the United States have shown that private electric scooters have replaced about 34\% of car trips, while electric bicycles have replaced between 5\% and 17\% of such trips \cite{liao_electric_2022}.
The high substitution rates observed for private electric micro-mobility suggest that the deployment of shared micro-mobility services can effectively encourage a modal shift from high-emission automobile travel to low-emission shared micro-mobility alternatives.

Micromobility sharing services try to increase user satisfaction to maximize profit.
Users of micromobility sharing services are often willing to walk a short distance to a sharing vehicle.
Demand that the sharing service has met, i.e., the sharing service could satisfy the user's demand by providing an available vehicle within the user's walking radius, is often referred to in the literature as met or satisfied demand.
Unmet or unsatisfied demand is user demand that the sharing service cannot satisfy, i.e., when a user cannot find an available vehicle within the walking radius.
This unmet demand is what sharing services try to minimize.
Balancing the fleet of a shared micromobility service reduces the amount of unmet demand and thereby increases profit \cite{liao_electric_2022}.

\subsection{Rebalancing}\label{sec:background-mss-rebalancing}
Rebalancing refers to the operation of moving vehicles from locations with more supply than demand to locations with less supply than expected demand.
The goal of rebalancing is to satisfy more demand by allocating vehicles to locations where they are more needed.
Rebalancing strategies often rely on forecasts of demand to anticipate areas with higher demand and relocate vehicles to these locations, which makes rebalancing strategies highly dependent on the quality of demand forecasts.

Rebalancing can broadly be classified as dynamic and static approaches.
Static rebalancing includes pre-planned or periodic rebalancing operations, which are typically based on historical demand data.
Static rebalancing operations are often done overnight to improve fleet distribution for the upcoming day.
This approach benefits from lower operational costs and management complexity, but it is not flexible and is incapable of reacting to demand fluctuations over the day.
Dynamic rebalancing, on the other hand, leverages real-time system data and short-term demand forecasts to do rebalancing operations during service hours.
This dynamic approach can reduce unsatisfied demand and therefore improve user satisfaction more effectively.
However, the operational costs for dynamic rebalancing are much higher.
Additionally, this approach requires continuous data collection and more advanced rebalancing strategies.
Hybrid schemes can combine the benefits of both while balancing operational cost and flexibility by using both static and dynamic rebalancing.

There exist numerous approaches to tackling rebalancing.
The most common approaches, user-based and operator-based rebalancing, are discussed in the following sections.

\subsubsection{User-based Rebalancing}
User-based rebalancing describes rebalancing operations done by the users of sharing services.
Mostly, users are offered an incentive, often as a monetary incentive or a bonus, to allocate vehicles to different locations.
This incentive is often done through recommendations of different pick-up and drop-off locations.
This user-based rebalancing highly depends on users' willingness to accept the incentive to reposition vehicles.
Therefore, the scope of rebalancing is somewhat limited to the users' willingness, which the operator can only partially influence.
User-based rebalancing is often cheaper than operator-based rebalancing.
Often, a combination of both user-based and operator-based rebalancing offers a good trade-off between the benefits and drawbacks of both types of rebalancing.

\subsubsection{Operator-based Rebalancing}
Operator-based rebalancing is often done through a crew of service workers, who pick-up vehicles with the help of trucks and drop them off at different locations.
These trucks are often equipped with battery charging technologies and/or spare batteries, which can replace the vehicles' low-charge batteries.
Operator-based rebalancing is often costly for the sharing service since service workers and equipment must be paid for.
Nevertheless, operator-based rebalancing can be much better controlled than user-based rebalancing since no willingness needs to be considered.

\section{Reinforcement Learning (RL)}\label{sec:background-reinforcement_learning}
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a machine learning technique where an agent interacts with an environment by choosing between multiple actions. 
This happens in a sequence.
After each time step, the agent decides between the actions to optimize the reward.
The agent gets information about the environment in which it acts through a state representation.
This state often simplifies the underlying problem that the agent tries to solve.
The state is time-dependent, so the state changes after each time step.
The general goal of the agent is to maximize the accumulated reward, which is often called return, through trial and error. 
The agent tries different actions depending on the current state and learns, which leads to more rewards.
The strategy by which the agent chooses its following action based on the current state is called policy \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.
\autoref{fig:mdp-diagram} illustrates this iterative approach of RL.

\subsection{Markov Decision Process (MDP) and Dynamic Programming (DP)}\label{sec:background-reinforcement_learning-mdp}
A fundamental framework in the field of RL is the Markov Decision Process (MDP).
An MDP is defined by a tuple consisting of four components (see equation \ref{eq:mdp}).
Decision-making in environments where outcomes are not entirely in control of the agent can be modeled using such an MDP.
Those MDPs can then be solved with dynamic programming (DP).
DP is a technique to solve such complex models by dividing the problem into multiple sub-problems and using estimates to find an optimal policy that solves the problem \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:mdp}
    MDP : (\mathcal{S}; \mathcal{A}; P; R)
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{S}$: A set of states representing the environment the RL agent is interacting with. This is often referred to as the state space.
    \item $\mathcal{A}$: Represents the set of actions the agent can choose from, which is often called the action space.
    \item $P(s' \mid s, a)$: The state transition probabilities are the probabilities for being in state $s$ and transitioning through action $a$ into the new state $s'$.
    \item $R(s, a, s')$: The reward function assigns each possible state transition a reward value. That is the reward when transitioning from state $s$ to state $s'$ by the action $a$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.62\linewidth]{content/figures/Markov_diagram.png}
    \caption{MDP as a diagram representation \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}}
    \label{fig:mdp-diagram}
\end{figure}

The Markov property is a fundamental assumption when working with MDPs.
This property states that the next state depends only on the current state and action, not on previous states.
This makes the problem to solve much simpler \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\subsubsection{Value Functions}
Key functions for solving MDPs are the state-value function presented in equation \ref{eq:state-value-function} and the action-value function presented in equation \ref{eq:action-value-function} \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

The state-value function represents the expected reward of being in state $s$ and acting under the policy $\pi$.
The action-value function, on the other hand, represents the expected reward of being in state $s$ doing the action $a$ and then acting with regards to the policy $\pi$ \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:state-value-function}
v_\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k R_{t+k+1} \,\middle|\, S_t = s \right]
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:action-value-function}
q_\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k R_{t+k+1} \,\middle|\, S_t = s, A_t = a \right]
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Bellman Equations}
The previously defined state-value and action-value functions describe the rewards for a given state, policy, and action well.
However, they are very complex to solve and require complete model knowledge.
Therefore, the Bellman equations describe an iterative approach for calculating the state-value function (see equation \ref{eq:bellman-action-value-function}) and the action-value function(see equation \ref{eq:bellman-state-value-function}) \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:bellman-state-value-function}
v_\pi(s) = \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \pi(a \mid s) \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma v_\pi(s') \right]
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:bellman-action-value-function}
q_\pi(s, a) = \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma \sum_{a' \in \mathcal{A}} \pi(a' \mid s') q_\pi(s', a') \right]
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Policy Iteration}
Policy iteration and value iteration are the two key algorithms DP uses to solve MDPs. They make use of the previously defined Bellman Equations and their recursive approach.
Policy Iteration consists of two steps: Policy Evaluation and Policy Improvement \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.
Policy Evaluation estimates how good the policy is in every state; therefore, the State-Value function (see equation \ref{eq:bellman-state-value-function}) is calculated for the current policy $\pi$ for every state.
After evaluating the current policy, the next step is to improve it, which is done by solving the equation \ref{eq:policy-improvement}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:policy-improvement}
\pi'(s) = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma v_\pi(s') \right]
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Value Iteration}
On the other hand, value iteration tries to solve MDPs by directly updating the value function instead of using the two-step process from policy iteration.
Value iteration repeatedly applies the Bellman optimality equation until this function converges and an optimal state-value function $v^*(s)$ is found (see equation \ref{eq:bellman-optimality}).
With this optimal state-value function, an optimal policy can be obtained with the equation \ref{eq:value-iteration-optimal-policy} \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:bellman-optimality}
    v_{k+1}(s) = \max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma v_k(s') \right]
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:value-iteration-optimal-policy}
\pi^*(s) = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma v^*(s') \right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Multi-Armed Bandits (MAB)}
The Multi-Armed Bandit theory is a simplified version of a reinforcement learning agent.
An MAB agent has k arms, each representing an action the agent can choose from.
Notably, the agent has no state representation of the environment it is interacting with.
This means the MAB agent can try out each action and choose based on the previous rewards from trying out the different actions.
That way, the MAB agent can learn the underlying probability distributions for the reward of each action.
When the MAB agent has explored the actions enough, he can exploit this information to maximize his total reward, which is the goal.
The MAB theory describes the trade-off between exploration and exploitation by examining the different actions to estimate their reward distributions.
In contrast, the agent uses his gained information to maximize his total reward \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.

The estimated reward for a given action $a$ can be calculated as the average reward for choosing that action.
This estimated reward is denoted as $Q_t(a)$ and is presented in equation \ref{eq:mab-sample-average} \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:mab-sample-average}
    Q_t(a) \doteq \frac{\text{sum of rewards when a taken prior to t}}{\text{number of times a taken prior to t}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{t-1} R_i \, \mathbf{1}_{\{A_i = a\}}s}{\sum_{i=1}^{t-1} \mathbf{1}_{\{A_i = a\}}}
\end{equation}

Numerous algorithms have been developed for MAB agents to balance exploration and exploitation.

\subsubsection{$\mathbf{\varepsilon}$-greedy}
The $\varepsilon$-greedy algorithm is a relatively simple approach to balance exploration and exploitation.
The agent chooses a random arm with the probability of $\varepsilon$.
In the other cases, e.g., $1-\varepsilon$, the agent exploits the current best arm, which has the highest estimated reward.
The algorithm is presented in equation \ref{eq:epsilon-greedy} \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:epsilon-greedy}
A_t =
\begin{cases}
\arg\max_{a} Q_t(a) & \text{with probability } 1-\epsilon, \\
\text{a random action uniformly chosen from } \mathcal{A} & \text{with probability } \epsilon.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Upper Confidence Bound (UCB)}
The Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) is an often-used algorithm to balance exploration and exploitation of MAB agents.
This approach chooses the arm based on the highest estimated reward and the uncertainty of those estimates.
Therefore, the UCB algorithm improves estimates based on the variance of each estimate.
The algorithm is presented in equation \ref{eq:upper-confidence-bound} \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:upper-confidence-bound}
A_t \doteq \arg\max_a \left[ Q_t(a) + c \sqrt{\frac{\ln t}{N_t(a)}} \right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Q-Learning}
Q-Learning is an often-used RL method that does not require complete knowledge of the environment and, therefore, is a model-free method. In Q-Learning, the approach is to optimize the quality function iteratively.
A state-value function and a policy can be derived from this optimized quality function.
Q-Learning is an off-policy algorithm, meaning two distinct policies are used.
This algorithm will optimize the target policy, and a different policy is used for exploration.
In contrast, on-policy algorithms like SARSA use one policy online, which is used for exploration and is the target policy to be optimized.
Equation \ref{eq:q-learning} shows the update rule for the Q-Learning algorithm, where $\alpha$ is the learning rate, $r$ is the immediate reward, and $s'$ is the next state.
With this update rule, the algorithm iteratively updates the action-value function by sampling transitions from state $s$ to state $s'$, until the algorithm converges to an optimal action-value function $q^*(s, a)$ \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:q-learning}
    q(s,a) \leftarrow q(s,a) + \alpha \left[ r + \gamma \max_{a'} q(s',a') - q(s,a) \right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Deep-Q-Networks (DQN)}\label{sec:background-reinforcement_learning-dqn}
Deep-Q-Networks (DQN) solve the problem of high-dimensional or large state spaces.
Traditional RL algorithms struggle with large state spaces and require lengthy and complex training.
In DQN, a multi-layer CNN (convolutional neural network) approximates the Q-value function $q(s, a;\theta)$, with network weights $\theta$.
The network inputs the state and outputs estimated Q-values for each possible action.
By training this network, the agent can generalize past experiences to new states \cite{arulkumaran_deep_2017}.

DQN became so popular due to several innovations in this field.
Experience Replay stabilizes training by, instead of updating from consecutive, correlated state transitions, DQN uses a replay memory to store the agent's experiences, e.g., transitions $(s_t, a_t, s_{t+1}, r_{t+1})$ at each time step.
From this memory, mini-batches are sampled uniformly to break temporal correlations between state transitions and stabilize training \cite{arulkumaran_deep_2017}.

A second network, the target network, can compute Q-learning target values.
These targets get computed after several iterations, reducing the fluctuation in these targets and stabilizing training \cite{arulkumaran_deep_2017}.

\subsection{Policy Gradient Methods}\label{sec:background-reinforcement_learning-pgm}
One limitation of the previously discussed RL methods is the constraint that the action space must be deterministic.
Value-based methods like Q-Learning estimate the optimal value function and derive it from their policy.
In contrast, policy gradient methods directly optimize the policy. Therefore, these methods use a parameterized policy whose parameters $\theta$ are iteratively updated in order to maximize rewards.
A performance measure is introduced to update these policy parameters, representing the actual value function for the policy $\pi_\theta$.
This performance function $J(\theta)$ is then differentiated by the policy parameters $\theta$ and can be used to update the policy in such a way as to optimize the value function and maximize rewards.
The update rule and performance measure are defined as follows \cite{sewak_policy-approximation_2022}:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:policy-gradient-update-rule}
    \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \nabla J(\theta)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:policy-gradient-performance-measure}
\nabla J(\theta) = \nabla v_{\pi_\theta}(s_0)
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Actor-critic Methods}
Policy gradient methods often struggle with high variance and sample inefficiency.
This leads to a slow and unstable learning process.
Therefore, actor-critic methods were introduced to address these issues with policy gradient methods. Actor-critic methods use two agents, one learning the policy called the "actor" and the "critic" learning to estimate the value function for the given policy.
In every step, both the "actor" and the "critic" receive the new state, and only the "critic" receives the reward.
The "critic" estimates the state-value function based on the reward and the state.
Then, the "actor" updates its policy based on the TD-Error between the "critic's" estimate and the reward.
The "critic" also updates its state value approximation based on this TD-Error.
Another widespread approach for Actor-critic methods is to use the advantage $A(s, a)=q(s, a) - v(s)$ as the feedback to the "actor", which stabilizes training even more \cite{sewak_policy-approximation_2022}.

\subsection{Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL)}
Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) describes a different approach to combining RL algorithms.
In HRL, the task is decomposed into subtasks that are much easier to solve.
Another benefit of HRL is that some subtasks are often more abstract representations and can be reused for similar use cases.
HRL uses Semi-Markov Decision Processes (SMDP) instead of regular MDPs due to the variability of SMDP to model actions of different levels of abstraction \cite{shubham_hierarchical_2024}.

A prevalent framework for HRL is the options framework from Sutton et al. \cite{sutton_between_1999}.
Whereby options generalize one-step actions into multi-step actions.
This means options consist of multiple actions chosen autonomously in a series.
The agent then learns a higher-level policy over options instead of actions.
The biggest challenge with this options framework is modeling the options.
Experts usually do this with a lot of domain knowledge.
Several approaches have been employed to learn such options, but they are not generally applicable and often fail to find optimal options.


\subsection{Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL)}
The previously discussed RL frameworks involve only one agent learning through interaction with the environment.
Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) extends this framework by introducing a setting involving multiple agents that interact or compete against each other.
This multi-agent approach increases the use case of RL for many real-world problems that involve not only one agent.
MARL involves a much more complex setting through the interaction of multiple agents.
Therefore, the state is not only influenced by the actions of one agent, but the agents need to account for changes in the state due to the actions of the other agents.
In competitive settings, the agents need a channel for communication.
This communication can be implicit through observation or explicit as part of the agent's actions \cite{busoniu_multi-agent_2008}.

MARL cannot be formalized with an MDP due to the lack of support for multiple agents.
Therefore, numerous approaches have been introduced to formalize MARL models.
A well-known approach is the Markov game (MG). 
In MG, each agent has its own set of actions and its reward function.
The state space and the state transition probabilities are shared between all agents.
The reward functions should be chosen according to the agents' interaction type.
A zero-sum reward is often used for competitive agents, where one's gain in reward is another's loss.
For a cooperative task, a shared reward is often recommended \cite{busoniu_multi-agent_2008}.

\subsection{Summary}
Multi-armed bandits, which address the exploration–exploitation dilemma in isolation without state transitions, offer an intuitive framework for understanding decision trade-offs.
However, due to the simplicity of these models, their application is limited to isolated decision-making problems.
Q-learning, a model-free and off-policy algorithm, learns optimal action value functions by iteratively updating estimates based on observed rewards.
While Q-learning is effective in discrete environments, it faces challenges in scalability and often suffers from overestimation bias.
DQN uses deep neural networks to approximate Q-values from high-dimensional inputs, improving generalization for complex real-world tasks at the cost of increased computational complexity.
In contrast, policy gradient methods optimize the policy directly and are particularly well-suited for continuous action spaces.
Their ability to learn stochastic policies is essential for adapting to rapidly changing environments. 

HRL further decomposes complex tasks into hierarchical subtasks through abstraction, allowing high-level policies to set sub-goals for lower-level controllers, which can improve learning efficiency. 
However, designing an effective hierarchy often requires additional human intervention and extensive optimization.
Finally, MARL extends these methodologies to settings involving multiple interacting agents, though it introduces additional challenges such as non-stationarity and scalability concerns.
In summary, the various reinforcement learning approaches discussed in this section provide a comprehensive foundation for optimizing shared micromobility fleets.
These approaches provide a balance between ease of use and the scalability and complexity required to effectively handle a complex and dynamic environment, found in shared micromobility systems.

\section{Heuristics}\label{sec:background-heuristics}
A heuristic is a strategy, similar to a rule of thumb, that tries to find a good solution fast.
Heuristics do not guarantee the finding of an optimal solution for a given problem.
The main advantage of heuristics is their performance.
The most common heuristic is the greedy heuristic, which greedily chooses the action with the most significant immediate reward, i.e., the optimal local choice.
Greedy does not consider previous decisions and treats every choice individually.
This simple approach makes greedy heuristics computationally efficient, but it lacks complexity, which leads to globally suboptimal solutions.
In contrast, greed-and-match enhances the simple greedy heuristic by incorporating a simple matching strategy.
This matching strategy assesses multiple decision elements and matches them to find more optimal solutions.
This addition of a matching strategy improves the overall solution created by the greed-and-match algorithm, leading to more globally optimal solutions than solutions from the simpler greedy algorithm.

\subsection{Metaheuristic Optimization}
Metaheuristics are frameworks that try to improve heuristics by guiding their actions to improve the search space and find more optimal solutions than can be found solely with the heuristics themselves.
Metaheuristics often generate multiple candidates and compare those to each other to find more optimal heuristics.
This is often done through physical processes like evolution or swarm behavior in a simulation environment.

\subsubsection{Genetic Algorithms (GAs)}
Genetic algorithms (GAs) use an evolutionary strategy to improve a population of candidate solutions iteratively. 
Each candidate is evaluated by a fitness function, which quantifies its suitability relative to the optimization objective. 
The algorithm generates a new population in each generation through three primary operators: selection, crossover, and mutation. 
Candidate solutions are probabilistically chosen during selection based on their fitness value, ensuring that higher-performing candidates are more likely to contribute to subsequent generations. 
The crossover operator then recombines elements from pairs of selected candidates to create new solutions. At the same time, the mutation operator randomly modifies individual candidates to preserve genetic diversity and prevent too-early convergence. 
This iterative process, involving the selection, crossover, and mutation operators, is repeated across multiple generations until the fitness values converge to a stable optimum or a pre-defined termination criterion is met.

\subsubsection{Ant Colony Optimization (ACO)}
Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) uses a different approach than GA.
ACO is a swarm intelligence algorithm that mimics the behavior of ants.
The problem for ACO is modeled as a Graph with different paths to find the optimal solutions.
The "Ants" try out different solution paths.
Good solution paths will be marked with more pheromones than bad-performing paths.
In new iterations, the "Ants" will follow paths with more pheromones but will also try out different paths to find more optimal solutions.
This graph representation of the problem makes ACO well-suited for routing problems or similar because this kind of problem can be naturally modeled as a graph.

\subsection{Summary}
Heuristic and metaheuristic methods offer different approaches to optimization problems, each with distinct advantages and limitations.
Greedy heuristics operate by making a locally optimal choice at each decision point, providing fast and computationally inexpensive solutions.
However, this results in suboptimal global performance.
Greed-and-match heuristics extend the greedy approach by incorporating matching strategies, which help improve overall solution quality, but also introduce additional computational overhead and complexity compared to simple greedy algorithms.
In contrast, GAs simulate the process of natural selection by evolving a population of candidate solutions.
GAs can explore larger search spaces by combining features from different solutions.
However, they often require significantly more computational resources to avoid premature convergence and maintain genetic diversity.
ACO mimics the pheromone-based foraging behavior of ant swarms, where artificial ants traverse a graph representing the problem space.
This method is particularly well-suited for routing and network design problems.
However, ACO can sometimes suffer from getting trapped in local optima, resulting in less optimal solutions.
In summary, while greedy and greed-and-match heuristics offer simplicity and speed, they may not be sufficient to address global optimization challenges.
Conversely, GAs and ACO provide more robust frameworks that are better equipped to handle complex, dynamic problem spaces, but at the cost of increased computational complexity.

\section{Mathematical Optimization}\label{sec:background-mathematical_optimization}
Mathematical optimization uses a drastically different approach from RL and heuristics.
Mathematical optimization approaches decision problems by formulating them as mathematical expressions consisting of objective functions and constraints that can be solved mathematically.
This approach contrasts the previously discussed RL and heuristic methods with the trial-and-error approach combined with simulation environments.
Formulating a mathematical expression comes with some limitations.
A simplified model of the environment is often formulated due to the high complexity of real-world scenarios, which can not be formulated as a mathematical expression in a feasible time.
Linear Programming (LP) and Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) are the most common approaches for problem formulation in the field of mathematical optimization.

\subsection{Linear Programming (LP)}
Linear programming (LP) is an optimization technique that is used to solve mathematical expressions for decision-making problems.
In the context of LP, objective functions and constraints are linear, and their variables are continuous.
This restriction, however, limits the scope of applications of the LP approach when confronted with complex real-world scenarios.
A variety of algorithms exist to solve LPs for optimal solutions, but the most common are the simplex algorithm and the interior-point method.
The simplex algorithm utilizes a convex-shaped polytope, defined by the intersections of the LP constraints.
The algorithm starts with a base solution located at one of the vertices of the polytope.
Thereafter, the algorithm systematically moves from one vertex to the adjacent vertex, which provides the most significant improvement in terms of the objective function.
This iterative process continues until no neighboring vertex improves the objective function value, at which point the algorithm achieves optimality.
In contrast, the interior-point method traverses the interior of the feasible region rather than on the edges or the vertices.
To ensure that the method remains within the boundaries of the formulated LP, it incorporates barrier functions into the objective function.
By calculating a gradient, the algorithm determines its traversal direction.
As the iterations progress, the influence of the barrier functions decreases to ensure convergence to an optimal solution.
The simplex algorithm is particularly well-suited for moderately sized LPs due to its potential for nonconvergence when applied to larger LPs.
In contrast, the interior-point method is a more robust alternative when scaled to large LPs with much more complex constraints to find an optimal solution.
While the interior-point method scales better, it comes with more computational overhead.
In summary, the simplex method is more suited for solving smaller or repetitive LPs, while the interior point method is better suited for larger and more complex LPs.


\subsection{Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP)}
Mixed integer linear programming (MILP) extends LP by allowing integer variables to be an alternative to continuous variables.
These integer variables allow modeling of binary variables (0/1) as well, which increases the usability of MILPs in contrast to LPs.
Branch-and-cut algorithms often solve MILP problems.
These algorithms solve numerous LP relaxations in order to prune impossible or inefficient branches.
These branch-and-cut algorithms work well for a moderately complex system and will find optimal solutions.
The computing time for such algorithms increases exponentially, which makes them unsuitable for highly complex systems with large numbers of equations to solve.
Nevertheless, MILP is still helpful for such highly complex systems.
They can be split into multiple subproblems or can be used in combination with heuristic approaches.
