\section{Micromobility Sharing Services}\label{sec:background-mss}
Shared micromobility is a transportation service offering users short-term rentals of light vehicles, such as electric scooters (e-scooters) and electric bikes (e-bikes), within a defined operational area.
Micromobility sharing services offer customers a flexible and environmentally friendly alternative to traditional individual transportation.
Due to their compactness, motor assistance, and sufficient speed for urban travel, e-scooters and e-bikes have become the predominant vehicles for these services \cite{liao_electric_2022}. 

Both vehicle types enjoy popularity, yet they serve distinct purposes.
E-scooters are typically used for shorter journeys, averaging around 1.8 kilometers, and are often associated with leisure activities, such as shopping or social events. E-bikes, in contrast, are generally preferred for slightly longer distances, averaging 2 kilometers, with commuting being their dominant use case \cite{liao_electric_2022}.

From an environmental perspective, e-scooters and e-bikes offer a promising path toward reducing urban carbon emissions and air pollution, especially when powered by renewable energy.
Recent studies in the United States have shown that private electric scooters have replaced approximately 34\% of car trips, while electric bicycles have replaced between 5\% and 17\% of such trips \cite{liao_electric_2022}.
The high substitution rates observed for private electric micro-mobility suggest that the deployment of shared micro-mobility services can effectively encourage a modal shift from high-emission automobile travel to low-emission shared micromobility alternatives.

The commercial success of a micromobility sharing service is closely tied to user satisfaction, which depends on vehicle availability.
Vehicle availability defines whether a trip request can be served or not.
Users of micromobility sharing services are generally willing to walk a certain distance to access an available vehicle.
This threshold is commonly referred to as the acceptable walking radius and depends on several factors, including the trip purpose and the intended trip length.
When a user finds an available vehicle within this acceptable walking area, their demand is considered met or satisfied.
Conversely, if no vehicle is available inside this area, the demand is considered unmet or unsatisfied.
Therefore, service providers' primary goal in enhancing user satisfaction is to minimize unmet demand, i.e., maximize satisfied demand.
Fleet rebalancing is a key strategy employed to meet this objective by aligning vehicle supply with user needs \cite{jaber_preferences_2023}.

\subsection{Rebalancing}\label{sec:background-mss-rebalancing}
Rebalancing is the process of repositioning vehicles from areas of high supply (or low demand) to areas of high demand (or low supply) to correct imbalances in the fleet distribution.
The primary goal of rebalancing is to increase satisfied demand by moving vehicles proactively to locations where they are most needed.
Rebalancing strategies often rely on demand forecasts to anticipate areas with higher imbalances between user demand and vehicle supply.
Consequently, rebalancing strategies are highly dependent on the accuracy of demand forecasts.
\\

In terms of scheduling rebalancing operations, these strategies are broadly categorized as dynamic and static:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Static rebalancing} includes pre-planned or periodic rebalancing operations, typically conducted overnight to improve fleet distribution for the upcoming day.
    This approach benefits from lower operational costs and management complexity.
    However, it is inflexible and incapable of reacting to demand fluctuations throughout the day.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic rebalancing}, on the other hand, leverages real-time system data and short-term demand forecasts to do rebalancing operations during service hours.
    This dynamic approach is more effective at reducing unmet demand and improving user satisfaction, but incurs significantly higher operational costs.
    Additionally, this approach requires continuous data collection and more advanced rebalancing strategies.
\end{itemize}

The two predominant methods for executing these strategies, user-based and operator-based rebalancing, are discussed in the following sections.

\subsubsection{User-based Rebalancing}
User-based rebalancing leverages users to perform rebalancing tasks.
Operators typically incentivize users, often with discounts or credits, to pick-up or drop-off vehicles at specific locations that help balance the fleet.
This method is cost-effective, but its efficacy is constrained by the users' willingness to adjust their routes in response to incentives.
Therefore, the operator's control over the outcome of user-based rebalancing is indirect and limited \cite{emami_integrated_2024}.

\subsubsection{Operator-based Rebalancing}
Operator-based rebalancing is performed by a dedicated crew of service workers who use specialized vehicles, such as vans or trucks, to perform relocations.
These trucks are often equipped with battery charging technologies and spare batteries, allowing them to perform maintenance tasks while relocating vehicles.
This method is more expensive than user-based rebalancing due to labor and equipment costs.
The primary advantage is the reliable control over the fleet distributions, as this method doesn't depend on user participation \cite{dellamico_bike_2014}.
Given the distinct advantages and disadvantages of each approach, service providers must determine the optimal mix of strategies for their specific operational context.
Typically, a combined approach creates efficient and robust solutions, but needs fine-tuning based on the specific requirements \cite{mahmoodian_design_2019}.

\section{Reinforcement Learning (RL)}\label{sec:background-reinforcement_learning}
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a machine learning paradigm in which an agent learns to make optimal decisions through sequential interactions with an environment.
At each discrete time step $t$, the agent observes the environment's state $\mathcal{S}$, selects an action from an available set $\mathcal{A}$, and receives a numerical reward $R$ as feedback.
The agent's objective is to maximize the cumulative reward, or return, over time.
This learning process is guided by a policy, denoted as $\pi$, which is a strategy that maps states to actions \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.
Through a process of trial and error, the agent iteratively refines its policy to discover which actions yield the most reward in particular states.
The agent-environment interaction loop is illustrated in \autoref{fig:mdp-diagram}.

\subsection{Markov Decision Process (MDP) and Dynamic Programming (DP)}\label{sec:background-reinforcement_learning-mdp}
A fundamental framework in the field of RL is the Markov Decision Process (MDP).
An MDP is defined by a tuple consisting of four components (see equation \ref{eq:mdp}).
Decision-making in environments where outcomes are not entirely in control of the agent can be modeled using such an MDP.
Problems formulated as MDPs can be solved using techniques such as dynamic programming (DP).
DP is a technique for solving complex models by dividing the problem into multiple sub-problems and using estimates to find an optimal policy that solves the problem \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:mdp}
    MDP : (\mathcal{S}; \mathcal{A}; P; R)
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{S}$: A finite set of states, known as the state space, representing all possible configurations of the environment.
    \item $\mathcal{A}$: A finite set of actions, known as the action space, that the agent can perform.
    \item $P(s' \mid s, a)$: The state transition function defines the probabilities for being in state $s$ and transitioning through action $a$ into the new state $s'$.
    \item $R(s, a, s')$: The reward function assigns each possible state transition a reward value. This immediate reward is given when transitioning from state $s$ to state $s'$ by the action $a$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.9\linewidth]{content/figures/Markov_diagram.png}
    \caption{The Reinforcement Learning Agent-Environment Interaction Loop. This diagram illustrates the fundamental cycle of a Markov Decision Process (MDP). At each discrete time step $t$, the agent observes the environment's current state ($\mathcal{S}_t$) and selects an action ($\mathcal{A}_t$). In response, the environment transitions to a new state ($\mathcal{S}_{t+1}$) and provides a numerical reward ($R_{t+1}$) to the agent. This feedback loop forms the basis of trial-and-error learning, as the agent's objective is to learn a policy that maximizes the cumulative reward over time \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.}
    \label{fig:mdp-diagram}
\end{figure}

The Markov property is a fundamental assumption when working with MDPs.
This property states that the next state depends only on the current state and the action taken, and not on the sequence of states and actions that preceded them
This assumption significantly simplifies the modeling process by ensuring that the current state provides all necessary information for optimal decision-making \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\subsubsection{Value Functions}
Key functions for solving MDPs are the state-value function presented in equation \ref{eq:state-value-function} and the action-value function presented in equation \ref{eq:action-value-function} \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

The state-value function represents the expected reward of being in state $s$ and following the policy $\pi$.
The action-value function, on the other hand, represents the expected reward of being in state $s$ performing the action $a$ and then acting to follow the policy $\pi$ \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:state-value-function}
v_\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k R_{t+k+1} \,\middle|\, S_t = s \right]
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:action-value-function}
q_\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k R_{t+k+1} \,\middle|\, S_t = s, A_t = a \right]
\end{equation}

Here, $\gamma \in [0, 1]$ is the discount factor, which balances the importance of immediate versus future rewards.

\subsubsection{Bellman Equations}
The previously defined state-value and action-value functions accurately describe the rewards associated with a given state, policy, and action.
However, solving these equations directly is computationally complex and requires a complete model of the environment's dynamics ($\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{R}$), which is often unavailable.
Therefore, the Bellman equations describe an iterative approach for calculating the state-value function (see equation \ref{eq:bellman-state-value-function}) and the action-value function (see equation \ref{eq:bellman-action-value-function}) \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.
They decompose the value of a state or state-action pair into the sum of the immediate reward and the discounted value of the successor states.
This recursive property is fundamental to many RL algorithms \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:bellman-state-value-function}
v_\pi(s) = \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \pi(a \mid s) \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma v_\pi(s') \right]
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:bellman-action-value-function}
q_\pi(s, a) = \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma \sum_{a' \in \mathcal{A}} \pi(a' \mid s') q_\pi(s', a') \right]
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Policy Iteration}
Dynamic Programming employs two main algorithms to solve MDPs: policy iteration and value iteration.
They utilize the previously defined Bellman Equations and their recursive approach.
Policy Iteration alternates between the two steps: Policy Evaluation and Policy Improvement \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.
Policy Evaluation estimates the effectiveness of the policy in every state.
Therefore, the State-Value function (see equation \ref{eq:bellman-state-value-function}) is calculated for the current policy $\pi$ for every state.
After evaluating the current policy, the next step is to improve it, which is done by solving the equation \ref{eq:policy-improvement}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:policy-improvement}
\pi'(s) = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma v_\pi(s') \right]
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Value Iteration}
On the other hand, value iteration attempts to solve MDPs by directly updating the value function, rather than using the two-step process employed in policy iteration.
Value iteration repeatedly applies the Bellman optimality equation until this function converges and an optimal state-value function $v^*(s)$ is found (see equation \ref{eq:bellman-optimality}).
With this optimal state-value function, an optimal policy can be obtained with the equation \ref{eq:value-iteration-optimal-policy} \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:bellman-optimality}
    v_{k+1}(s) = \max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma v_k(s') \right]
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:value-iteration-optimal-policy}
\pi^*(s) = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a) \left[ R(s, a, s') + \gamma v^*(s') \right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Multi-Armed Bandits (MAB)}
The Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) is a simplified RL problem that lacks states, forcing the agent to focus exclusively on the exploration-exploitation dilemma.
An MAB agent has k arms, each representing an action the agent can choose from.
Notably, the agent has no state representation of the environment it is interacting with.
This means the MAB agent can try out each action and choose based on the previous rewards it has received from exploring the different actions.
That way, the MAB agent can learn the underlying probability distributions for the reward of each action.
When the MAB agent has explored the actions sufficiently, it can exploit this information to maximize its total reward.
The MAB theory describes the trade-off between exploration and exploitation by exploring the different actions to estimate their reward distributions.
The agent uses the information it has gained to maximize its total reward, which is its primary goal \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.

The estimated reward for a given action $a$ can be calculated as the average reward for choosing that action.
This estimated reward is denoted as $Q_t(a)$ and is presented in equation \ref{eq:mab-sample-average} \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:mab-sample-average}
    Q_t(a) \doteq \frac{\text{sum of rewards when a taken prior to t}}{\text{number of times a taken prior to t}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{t-1} R_i \, \mathbf{1}_{\{A_i = a\}}s}{\sum_{i=1}^{t-1} \mathbf{1}_{\{A_i = a\}}}
\end{equation}

Numerous algorithms have been developed for MAB agents to balance exploration and exploitation.

\subsubsection{$\mathbf{\varepsilon}$-greedy}
The $\varepsilon$-greedy algorithm is a relatively straightforward approach to strike a balance between exploration and exploitation.
The agent chooses a random arm with the probability of $\varepsilon$.
In the other cases, e.g., $1-\varepsilon$, the agent exploits the current best arm, which has the highest estimated reward.
The algorithm is presented in equation \ref{eq:epsilon-greedy} \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}, where $c$ controls the trade-off between exploration and exploitation.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:epsilon-greedy}
A_t =
\begin{cases}
\arg\max_{a} Q_t(a) & \text{with probability } 1-\epsilon, \\
\text{a random action uniformly chosen from } \mathcal{A} & \text{with probability } \epsilon.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Upper Confidence Bound (UCB)}
The Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) is a widely used algorithm for balancing exploration and exploitation in MAB settings.
This approach selects the arm based on the highest estimated reward and the uncertainty associated with those estimates.
Therefore, the UCB algorithm improves estimates based on the variance of each estimate.
The algorithm is presented in equation \ref{eq:upper-confidence-bound} \cite{sutton_reinforcement_2018}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:upper-confidence-bound}
A_t \doteq \arg\max_a \left[ Q_t(a) + c \sqrt{\frac{\ln t}{N_t(a)}} \right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Q-Learning}
Q-learning is a foundational model-free RL algorithm, meaning it can learn an optimal policy without requiring a model of the environment's dynamics ($P$ and $R$).
Q-Learning is an off-policy algorithm, meaning two distinct policies are used.
This algorithm optimizes the target policy, while a different behavior policy is used for exploration.
In contrast, on-policy algorithms like SARSA use a single policy, which is used for exploration and serves as the target policy to be optimized.
In Q-Learning, the approach is to optimize the quality function iteratively.
A state-value function and a policy can be derived from this optimized quality function.
Equation \ref{eq:q-learning} defines the update rule for the Q-Learning algorithm, where $\alpha$ is the learning rate, $r$ is the immediate reward, and $s'$ is the next state.
With this update rule, the algorithm iteratively updates the action-value function by sampling transitions from state $s$ to state $s'$, until the algorithm converges to an optimal action-value function $q^*(s, a)$ \cite{zhang_dive_2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:q-learning}
    q(s,a) \leftarrow q(s,a) + \alpha \left[ r + \gamma \max_{a'} q(s',a') - q(s,a) \right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Deep-Q-Networks (DQN)}\label{sec:background-reinforcement_learning-dqn}
Traditional Q-learning struggles with high-dimensional or continuous state spaces, as maintaining a tabular representation of Q-values becomes infeasible.
Deep Q-Networks (DQN) address this problem by using neural networks instead.
In DQN, a deep neural network approximates the Q-value function $q(s, a;\theta)$, with network weights $\theta$.
The network takes the state as input and outputs estimated Q-values for each possible action.
By training this network, the agent can generalize past experiences to new unseen states \cite{mnih_human-level_2015}.

DQN gained popularity due to several key innovations in this field, which massively increased the usability of DQN.
Experience replay stabilizes training by using a replay memory to store the agent's experiences (e.g., transitions $(s_t, a_t, s_{t+1}, r_{t+1})$ at each time step), instead of learning from consecutive, correlated state transitions.
From this memory, mini-batches are sampled uniformly to break temporal correlations between state transitions, which stabilizes training \cite{mnih_human-level_2015}.

Double DQN (DDQN) introduces a second network.
This second network, the target network, can compute Q-learning target values.
These targets are computed after several iterations, which reduces fluctuations in these targets and stabilizes training \cite{hasselt_deep_2016}.

\subsection{Policy Gradient Methods}\label{sec:background-reinforcement_learning-pgm}
In contrast to value-based methods, such as Q-Learning, which estimate the optimal value function and derive it from their policy, policy gradient methods directly optimize the policy.
Therefore, these methods utilize a parameterized policy whose parameters, $\theta$, are iteratively updated to maximize rewards.
A performance measure is introduced to update these policy parameters, representing the actual value function for the policy $\pi_\theta$.
This performance function $J(\theta)$ is then differentiated by the policy parameters $\theta$ and can be used to update the policy in such a way as to optimize the value function and maximize rewards.
This approach is particularly effective for continuous or high-dimensional action spaces and can learn stochastic policies naturally \cite{sewak_policy-approximation_2022}.
The update rule and performance measure are defined as follows:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:policy-gradient-update-rule}
    \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \nabla_\theta J(\theta_t)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:policy-gradient-performance-measure}
\nabla_\theta J(\theta_t) = \nabla v_{\pi_\theta}(s_0)
\end{equation}

Where $\nabla_\theta J(\theta_t)$ is the policy gradient, which indicates the direction of the steepest increase in expected return.

\subsubsection{Actor-critic Methods}
Standard policy gradient methods often struggle with high variance in the gradient estimates and overall sample inefficiency.
This leads to a slow and unstable learning process.
Therefore, actor-critic methods were introduced to address these issues of standard policy gradient methods.
Actor-critic methods utilize two components: the \textit{actor} learns the policy, and the \textit{critic} learns to estimate the value function for the given policy.
In every step, both the \textit{actor} and the \textit{critic} receive the new state, and only the \textit{critic} receives the reward.
The \textit{critic} estimates the state-value function based on the reward and the state.
Then, the \textit{actor} updates its policy based on the Temporal Difference Error (TD-Error) between the \textit{critic's} estimate and the reward.
The \textit{critic} also updates its state-value approximation based on this TD-Error.
A widespread approach for actor-critic methods is to use the advantage $A(s, a)=q(s, a) - v(s)$ as the feedback to the \textit{actor}, which results in lower variance and even more stable training \cite{sewak_policy-approximation_2022}.

\subsubsection{Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)}
Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is a state-of-the-art actor-critic method designed to enhance training stability and sample efficiency.
PPO achieves this by optimizing a clipped surrogate objective function.
This objective function constrains the policy update in a simple way to not differ too much from the previous policy.
This avoids destructively large policy updates, making PPO more robust and easier to implement than other methods while maintaining state-of-the-art performance \cite{schulman_proximal_2017}.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL)}
The previously discussed RL algorithms involve only one agent learning through interaction with the environment.
Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) extends this by introducing a setting involving multiple agents that interact or compete against each other.
MARL involves a much more complex setting through the interaction of multiple agents.
Therefore, the state is not only influenced by the actions of one agent, but the agents need to account for changes in the state due to the actions of the other agents.
Often, a channel for communication is established between the agents.
This communication can be implicit through observation or explicit as part of the agent's actions \cite{busoniu_multi-agent_2008}.

An MDP cannot formalize MARL due to the lack of support for multiple agents.
Therefore, numerous approaches have been introduced to formalize MARL models.
A well-known approach is the Markov Game (MG). 
In MG, each agent has its own set of actions and its own reward function.
The state space and the state transition probabilities are shared between all agents.
The reward functions should be chosen based on the type of interaction between the agents.
A zero-sum reward is often used for competitive agents, where one's gain in reward is another's loss.
For a cooperative task, a shared reward is often recommended \cite{busoniu_multi-agent_2008}.


\subsection{Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL)}
Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) addresses the challenges of solving complex, long-horizon tasks by decomposing them into a hierarchy of simpler sub-problems.
Instead of learning a single, monolithic policy to map high-level states to low-level actions, HRL introduces multiple levels of control.
The most common structure is a two-level hierarchy consisting of a high-level policy (often called a manager or meta-controller) and one or more low-level policies (often called workers or controllers).
The high-level policy operates at a coarse temporal scale, observing the environment's state and selecting a \textit{goal} or sub-task for the low-level policy to accomplish.
The low-level policy then operates at a finer temporal scale, taking primitive actions in the environment to achieve the specified goal, after which control returns to the high-level policy.
This division of labor allows the framework to learn policies at different levels of abstraction simultaneously \cite{pateria_hierarchical_2021}.

This hierarchical decomposition yields several significant benefits, most notably temporal abstraction and improved exploration.
Temporal abstraction enables the high-level policy to make decisions over extended periods without needing to specify every primitive action, thereby drastically reducing the complexity of the planning horizon.
For instance, a high-level rebalancing agent might decide to "increase vehicle supply in region X," leaving the low-level policy to execute the sequence of actions required to achieve that goal.
This also leads to more efficient exploration; the low-level policy can learn meaningful, short-term skills (e.g., "pick-up a scooter" or "travel to a specific location"), while the high-level policy explores more strategically in the space of sub-goals rather than in the vast space of primitive actions.
This structured exploration is often more effective and leads to faster convergence.
While powerful, the design of HRL frameworks introduces its own set of challenges \cite{pateria_hierarchical_2021}.

A foundational approach to formalizing this hierarchy is the options framework introduced by Sutton et al. \cite{sutton_between_1999}, where multi-step actions, or \textit{options,} are learned as temporally extended sub-policies.
However, a key challenge in HRL is defining these subtasks or goals.
In many cases, they must be predefined by a human expert with significant domain knowledge.
While research into automatic goal discovery exists, it remains an open problem.
Furthermore, designing the reward structure for both levels of the hierarchy is a non-trivial task.
The low-level policy requires intrinsic motivation (rewards for achieving goals), whereas extrinsic rewards from the environment drive the high-level policy.
Despite these challenges, HRL provides a powerful paradigm for tackling problems with a clear hierarchical structure, such as the strategic and operational decisions involved in fleet optimization.

\subsection{Summary}
This section has provided an overview of Reinforcement Learning, from its theoretical foundations in Markov Decision Processes to advanced, practical algorithms.
Multi-armed bandits, which address the exploration–exploitation dilemma in isolation without state transitions, offer an intuitive framework for understanding decision trade-offs.
However, due to the simplicity of these models, their application is limited to isolated decision-making problems.
Q-learning, a model-free and off-policy algorithm, learns optimal action value functions by iteratively updating estimates based on observed rewards.
While Q-learning is effective in discrete environments, it faces challenges in scalability and often suffers from overestimation bias.
DQN utilizes deep neural networks to approximate Q-values from high-dimensional inputs, thereby enhancing generalization for complex real-world tasks at the expense of increased computational complexity.
In contrast, policy gradient methods optimize the policy directly and are particularly well-suited for continuous action spaces.
Their ability to learn stochastic policies is essential for adapting to rapidly changing environments.

MARL extends these methodologies to settings involving multiple interacting agents, though it introduces additional challenges such as non-stationarity and scalability problems.
Finally, HRL further decomposes complex tasks into hierarchical subtasks through abstraction, allowing high-level policies to set sub-goals for lower-level controllers, thereby improving learning efficiency. 
However, designing an effective hierarchy often requires additional human intervention and extensive optimization.

In summary, the various reinforcement learning approaches discussed in this section provide a comprehensive foundation for optimizing shared micromobility fleets.
These approaches strike a balance between ease of use and the scalability and complexity necessary for effectively handling the complex, dynamic environments of shared micromobility systems.


\section{Heuristics}\label{sec:background-heuristics}
A heuristic is a problem-solving approach, similar to a rule of thumb, that attempts to find a satisfactory solution fast.
This is particularly relevant for complex problems, where finding an exact optimal solution is computationally impossible or too time-consuming.
While heuristics offer such a significant computational performance advantage, they do not guarantee global optimality. 

The most common heuristic is the greedy heuristic, which operates by making the locally optimal choice at each step, i.e., greedily choosing the action with the most significant immediate reward.
Greedy treats every choice individually, without considering future or previous decisions.
This simple approach makes the greedy heuristic computationally efficient.
However, it lacks complexity, resulting in globally suboptimal solutions.
Greed-and-match enhances this by incorporating a matching strategy, which considers relationships between multiple elements simultaneously.
For example, matching a set of supply locations to a set of demand locations.
This allows for more informed local choices, which often yield better overall solutions than a simple greedy algorithm but come at the cost of increased computational complexity \cite{wu_challenges_2020}.

\subsection{Metaheuristic Optimization}
Metaheuristics are a class of optimization techniques that aim to enhance heuristics by guiding their actions to improve the search space and discover more optimal solutions than can be found solely with the heuristics themselves.
Metaheuristics manage the trade-off between exploration and exploitation to overcome local optima and discover more optimal solutions.
These methods are often inspired by natural processes, such as evolution or swarm intelligence, and typically operate on a population of candidate solutions.

\subsubsection{Genetic Algorithms (GAs)}
Genetic Algorithms (GAs) are a class of metaheuristics inspired by the principles of biological evolution.
GAs improve a population of candidate solutions iteratively to identify a near-optimal solution.
Each candidate is evaluated by a fitness function, which quantifies its suitability relative to the optimization objective. 
The algorithm generates a new population in each generation through three primary operators: 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Selection:} Candidate solutions are probabilistically chosen during selection based on their fitness value, ensuring that higher-performing candidates are more likely to contribute to subsequent generations. 
    \item \textbf{Crossover:} The crossover operator then recombines elements from pairs of selected candidates to create new solutions.
    \item \textbf{Mutation:} At the same time, the mutation operator randomly modifies individual candidates to preserve genetic diversity and prevent premature convergence. 
\end{itemize}

This iterative process, involving the selection, crossover, and mutation operators, is repeated across multiple generations until the fitness function converges to a stable optimum or a predefined termination criterion is met \cite{holland_adaptation_1992}.

\subsubsection{Ant Colony Optimization (ACO)}
Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) is a metaheuristic based on swarm intelligence that mimics the foraging behavior of ants.
The optimization problem is modeled as a graph, where the nodes represent the problem's components and the edges represent possible connections or choices.
Artificial \textit{ants} then traverse this graph to construct candidate solutions.
As the ants build these solutions, they deposit artificial pheromones along their chosen paths.

Paths corresponding to superior solutions receive more potent pheromones.
In subsequent iterations, the ants are probabilistically attracted to paths with higher concentrations of pheromones, thereby exploiting known good routes.
At the same time, a degree of stochastic exploration is maintained to allow the ants to discover novel paths.
This mechanism makes ACO well-suited for solving routing and scheduling problems, as well as other combinatorial optimization problems that can be naturally represented as graphs \cite{dorigo_ant_1997}.

\subsection{Summary}
Heuristic and metaheuristic methods offer a range of approaches to optimization problems, each with its distinct advantages and limitations.
Simple heuristics, such as the greedy heuristic, operate by making a locally optimal choice at each decision point, providing fast and computationally inexpensive solutions.
However, this results in suboptimal global performance.
Greed-and-match heuristics improve upon this by incorporating matching strategies, which help enhance overall solution quality.
However, they also introduce additional computational overhead and complexity compared to simple heuristics.

In contrast, metaheuristics such as GAs and ACO offer more robust frameworks for navigating complex search spaces.
GAs leverage evolutionary principles to evolve a population of solutions.
ACO mimics the pheromone-based foraging behavior of ant swarms, where artificial ants traverse a graph representing the problem space.
However, they often require significantly more computational resources to avoid premature convergence and maintain genetic diversity.

In summary, while greedy and greed-and-match heuristics offer simplicity and speed, they may not be sufficient to address global optimization challenges.
Conversely, GAs and ACO provide more robust frameworks that are better equipped to handle complex, dynamic problem spaces, but at the cost of a significant increase in computational complexity.

\section{Mathematical Optimization}\label{sec:background-mathematical_optimization}
Mathematical optimization uses a fundamentally different approach than RL and heuristics.
Mathematical optimization approaches problems by formulating them as mathematical expressions consisting of objective functions and constraints that can be solved mathematically.
This approach contrasts with the previously discussed RL and heuristic methods, employing a trial-and-error approach.
Formulating a mathematical expression based on real-world problems comes with some challenges and limitations.
A simplified model of the environment is often formulated due to the high complexity of real-world scenarios, which cannot be formulated as a mathematical expression in a feasible amount of time.
Linear Programming (LP) and Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) are the most common approaches for formulating problems in the field of mathematical optimization.

\subsection{Linear Programming (LP)}
Linear programming (LP) is an optimization technique that is used to solve mathematical expressions for decision-making problems.
In the context of LP, objective functions and constraints are linear, and their variables are continuous.
This restriction, however, limits the scope of applications of the LP approach when confronted with complex real-world scenarios.
A variety of algorithms exist to solve LPs for optimal solutions, but the most common are the simplex algorithm and the interior-point method.

\begin{itemize}
    \item The simplex algorithm utilizes a convex-shaped polytope, defined by the intersections of the LP constraints.
    The algorithm starts with a base solution located at one of the vertices of the polytope.
    Thereafter, the algorithm systematically moves from one vertex to the adjacent vertex, which provides the most significant improvement in terms of the objective function.
    This iterative process continues until no neighboring vertex improves the objective function value, at which point the algorithm achieves optimality \cite{boyd_convex_2004}.
    \item The interior-point method takes a different path, traversing through the interior of the feasible region.
    It uses barrier functions to ensure it remains within the constraint boundaries and calculates a gradient to determine the direction of traversal.
    As the algorithm progresses, the influence of the barriers is reduced, allowing convergence to an optimal solution \cite{boyd_convex_2004}.
\end{itemize}

While the simplex algorithm is efficient in practice for many problems, its worst-case time complexity can be exponential. In contrast, interior-point methods have polynomial-time complexity, making them more robust for very large-scale LPs.
While the interior-point method scales better, it comes with more computational overhead.
In summary, the simplex method is more suited for solving smaller or repetitive LPs, while the interior-point method is better suited for larger and more complex LPs \cite{boyd_convex_2004}.


\subsection{Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP)}
Mixed integer linear programming (MILP) is an extension of linear programming (LP) that allows some decision variables to be restricted to integer values.
This greatly enhances the modeling capabilities of the framework, enabling the direct representation of discrete choices, such as binary (0/1) decisions.

MILP problems are NP-hard and are typically solved using algorithms such as branch-and-cut.
This method solves a series of LP relaxations, where the integer constraints are temporarily ignored, to establish bounds on the optimal solution.
The algorithm then systematically branches on integer variables, creating a tree of subproblems.
It uses \textit{cuts}, or additional constraints, to prune branches of the tree that cannot yield an optimal solution.
While effective for moderately complex problems, the computational time of these algorithms can grow exponentially with problem size, making them unsuitable for finding exact solutions to very large or time-sensitive systems.
For such systems, however, MILP can still be applied to subproblems or used with heuristic methods to find high-quality approximate solutions \cite{vielma_mixed_2015}.
