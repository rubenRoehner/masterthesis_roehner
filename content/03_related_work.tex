\chapter{Related Work}\label{sec:related-work} % approx. 13 pages
The literature review presented in this chapter is built upon a systematic approach to identify, organize, and review the relevant existing research in the domain of rebalancing strategies in shared micromobility systems.
Initially, a comprehensive search was conducted using primarily Google Scholar with keywords like \textit{micromobility}, \textit{fleet optimization}, \textit{rebalancing}, \textit{e-scooter sharing}, and \textit{bike sharing}. 
This primary research was further enhanced by examining the citations of the previously retrieved articles.
Approximately 70 research papers have been found throughout this initial searching process.

A structured summarizing process has been used to manage this large number of research papers.
OpenAI's ChatGPT \textit{o3-mini-high} LLM has been used to summarize each research paper by using a standardized prompt and the research paper's PDF file.
These generated summaries enabled an initial assessment of the relevance of each scientific paper.
This reduced the number of relevant research papers to around 25.

Relevant papers were further organized and categorized using Zotero and an integration with Notion. 
This categorization resulted in a structured table classifying literature based on key attributes, including the specific sharing system examined and the rebalancing mode used. 
The sharing systems considered are e-scooter sharing (ESS) and bike sharing (BSS), and the defined rebalancing modes are operator-based (OB), user-based (UB), crowdsourcing (CS), and autonomous (AU).

Through the review process, three primary categories emerged for rebalancing algorithms: Reinforcement Learning, mathematical optimization techniques, and heuristic-based approaches. 
Each of these categories is explored individually in the following sections, where the most relevant contributions are summarized.
Moreover, tables are provided listing significant papers published between 2018 and 2025 within each category.
Subsequently, a section focuses on the simulation environments implemented to evaluate rebalancing strategies.
Finally, the literature review concludes with a subsection that summarizes the findings and identifies the remaining gaps in the literature.

\section{Mathematical Optimization Approaches}\label{sec:related-work_mathematical-optimization}

Lee et al. \cite{lee_battery_2024} present a study on operational management of free-floating shared e-scooter systems addressing the problems of battery swapping, rebalancing, and staff routing. 
The authors formulate a MILP problem consisting of the decision variables: which scooters to service, whether to swap the battery of a given e-scooter, the routing decisions of the rebalancing staff, and the number of e-scooters relocating between zones. 
This MILP problem aims to maximize total profit, which is calculated by the revenue generated by fulfilled user demand and the costs incurred by rebalancing and battery swapping actions. 
The MILP includes several constraints: routing viability, staff limitations, and vehicle limitations.

To solve the MILP problem, a Clustered Iterative Construction Approach (CICA) is proposed. 
This CICA is a heuristic solution that splits the calculation into two phases. This is done due to the complexity of this large-scale MILP problem. 
The first phase of this CICA clusters multiple service area regions, each associated with one staff member, to decompose the complexity. 
A minimal spanning tree (MST) approximates routing costs for traveling between areas, and the operational costs inside each region are approximated by the number of battery swaps and rebalancing operations based on the state of charge and regional demand. 
Imbalances between available scooters and demand are incorporated as well by a penalty. The second phase is split into three steps. 
In the first step, the algorithm generates an initial sequence of regions to visit for each cluster. 
In the second step, the algorithm assigns specific operations for each of the previously generated sequences for each stop, such as pick-up, drop-off, and battery swap. 
This is done iteratively until the constraints are exceeded or the estimated demand is fully met. 
In the last step, the sequences with the highest profit are chosen. 
The results show that the proposed CICA algorithm consistently delivers near-optimal solutions, outperforming baselines in effectiveness and efficiency through reduced computational times.\\

Saum et al. \cite{saum_optimizing_2024} propose a framework for optimizing dockless-shared e-scooter rebalancing operations utilizing machine learning techniques in combination with mathematical optimization. 
Their rebalancing approach builds upon a Gradient boosting demand prediction model. 
This prediction model forecasts the net trip gap between pick-up and drop-off. 
The parameters of this Gradient boosting regressor are optimized using Bayesian optimization. 
To generate different demand scenarios, a Monte Carlo simulation is used in combination with a  Seasonal Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (SGARCH) framework. 

The rebalancing problem is formulated as an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) model. 
This ILP model aims to identify optimal routes to allocate e-scooters, which service workers do. 
A generalized cost function is introduced based on the driving distance, pick-up and drop-off costs, and penalties for unmet demand and overcrowding. 
The algorithm accounts for different e-scooter states, such as a low battery to trigger recharging, faulty e-scooters to replace, and usable e-scooters that can be rebalanced. 
For small-scale problems, the ILP is solved using the GNU linear programming kit (GLPK). 
At the same time, more significant instances are addressed using a hybrid Ant Colony Optimization–ILP (ACO–ILP) algorithm. 
This hybrid approach consists of two stages. 
The ACO generates promising routes in the first stage based on pheromone trails and visibility factors. 
In the second stage, the ILP solver optimizes the pick-up and drop-off operations.
This work proposes a more complex demand forecasting model, a Monte Carlo simulation, and SGARCH. 
However, this approach heavily depends on historical data, which does not account for demand dynamics and fluctuations and only provides a static planning horizon. 
This limits the ability to react to unpredictable demand.
\\

Emami and Ramezani \cite{emami_integrated_2024} provide a user- and operator-based rebalancing framework for an e-bike sharing service operating in Manhattan, USA. 
Operator-based rebalancing includes rebalancing through a crew of trucks to move e-bikes from low-demand to high-demand areas and battery-swap operations to recharge batteries on the go.
The user-based rebalancing through monetary incentives only covers recommendations to drop-off e-bikes in low-demand areas while excluding pick-up recommendations.
The probability of users choosing to drop-off at a different spot than anticipated is derived using a willingness function. 
This willingness function quantifies the gain a user gets from dropping off at a particular location instead of taking the anticipated route. 
This willingness function includes incentives, additional walking time, and designated drop-off locations.
Using a logit model, this willingness function calculates the probability that the user is willing to accept the offered incentive.
Emami and Ramezani modeled the problem as a mixed binary rebalancing problem (MBRP).
The goal of this model is to minimize the objective function, which comprises truck travel costs, battery swapping costs, user incentive expenses, and penalties for unmet demand.
This MBRP is formulated as a mixed binary linear program (MBLP).
The paper proposes a branch-and-bound algorithm to solve the MBLP, resulting in a near-optimal solution.
The primary advantage of this integrated approach is that it combines user-based and operator-based rebalancing within a single framework, whereas existing literature typically covers these approaches in isolation.
In the simulation, the authors use real taxi demand data from Manhattan, applying random adjustments to the predicted locations and fluctuations in demand volume to account for uncertainty and real-world variability.
The use of taxi demand data can impact the effectiveness of the proposed rebalancing system, as taxi demand can vary significantly from the demand for shared e-bikes.
The proposed rebalancing framework is compared to three baselines: No charging – no rebalancing (NC-NR), Operator-based charging – no rebalancing (OC-NR), and Operator-based charging and rebalancing (OC-OR). A proper comparison of different rebalancing algorithms, including previous research, is missing.
\\

Rebalancing algorithms based on mathematical optimization techniques predominantly focus on operator-based rebalancing, specifically on the routing problem of service vehicles.
Such approaches often struggle with larger sharing systems, so most research uses a simplified representation of the sharing system and sometimes focuses only on a part of the operational area. 
The problem formulation is often done using MILP or LP, which is then solved using algorithms such as Branch and Bound (BB), interior point method (IPM), Coin-or Branch and Cut (CBC), or similar.
Some of the conducted research uses a combination of traditional solving algorithms and heuristics, which opens possibilities for enhancing the problem definition for more complex models.
However, they depend on static demand forecasts, simplified system representations, and fixed rebalancing intervals.
These issues limit such systems in terms of scalability and responsiveness to real-time demand fluctuations.
To bridge these gaps, future work should employ a dynamic and scalable framework that optimizes both operator-based and user-based rebalancing.
Focusing on capturing demand more dynamically and decomposing the rebalancing problem to improve scalability.

\section{Heuristic-based Approaches}\label{sec:related-work_heuristics}

Shen et al. \cite{shen_enhancing_2024} propose a framework for evaluating and optimizing the greenhouse gas emissions of shared e-bike systems, both docked and dockless variants. 
Part of their work is a rebalancing algorithm based on Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) that focuses on optimizing routes for their service vehicle. 
Initially, the framework uses hierarchical clustering and a Voronoi diagram to create zones based on demand similarity. 
The rebalancing problem is decomposed into two problems, one for intra-zone routing and the other for inter-zone routing. 
In the first phase, which focuses on intra-zone rebalancing, the algorithms determine pick-up and drop-off points based on the current number of available bikes and the expected demand for the next day. 
This is done for each zone, treating the geometric center of the zone as the pick-up and drop-off points as a Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) that ACO solves. 
The second phase of this algorithm deals with routing service vehicles between zones. 
In this phase, the ACO determines the optimal path for visiting each zone.
While this two-phase algorithm provides a robust mechanism for real-time fleet reallocation, the paper acknowledges its computational intensity.
The authors suggest that the high computational time demonstrated on high-end servers may pose a significant challenge when scaling the system to larger urban areas or integrating extended datasets.
Another weakness of this rebalancing approach is the extensive use of static parameter settings, such as acceptable walking distance or demand rate.
The use of static parameters affects the ability of this rebalancing framework to respond to fluctuations typical of real-world micromobility services.
\\

Kim et al. \cite{kim_optimal_2023} developed a near-optimal rebalancing strategy using a Genetic algorithm (GA) approach. 
The proposed system builds upon previous work on rebalancing bike-sharing systems and enhancing them to perform similarly for e-scooter-sharing systems. 
The authors begin by formulating the rebalancing problem as an MILP that seeks to distribute the available e-scooters to meet the target demand.
The primary goal of this algorithm is to strike a balance between supply and demand.
If that goal is met, the agents try to minimize the expected rebalancing time.
The target demand is defined by the forecasted demand during the morning peak, and the rebalancing is performed in a low-demand period in the early morning, before the morning peak. 
Therefore, this approach does not involve dynamic rebalancing throughout the day and cannot account for unexpected changes in demand.
The algorithm employs the operators selection (using roulette selection), crossover (using sequential recombination methods), and mutation to balance exploration and exploitation.
The authors employed detailed parameter tuning, adjusting the initial population size, number of generations, and other relevant parameters to ensure rapid convergence and near-optimal solutions.
A comparative analysis reveals that the GA-based algorithm outperforms traditional solvers, such as CPLEX, by significantly reducing computational time.
The algorithm efficiently rebalances e-scooters to match demand and supply with fewer rebalancing vehicles.
However, for large-scale sharing systems, the computational time of this GA approach increases exponentially, which makes it unattainable for such systems to perform in real-time.
Another limitation of this work is the simplified model of the sharing system. 
Euclidean distances, constant vehicle speed, and similar simplifications are employed, which may impact accuracy.
The evaluation process is conducted using a series of numerical experiments based on historical demand data, and the results are compared to those of the CPLEX algorithm as a baseline.
This limited evaluation lacks realism and fails to capture fluctuations or delays that occur in real-world sharing systems.
\\

Tolomei et al. \cite{tolomei_benefits_2021} propose a data-informed framework to optimize e-scooter sharing fleets. 
Their framework consists of two main components: a predictive demand model and a relocation heuristic. 
The e-scooter trip data is open data from the US cities of Louisville and Austin. 
Two distinct demand models are employed—a baseline model based on historical trip data using averages segmented by day and hour. 
Furthermore, a more advanced 3D-CLoST model consists of a convolutional neural network (CNN) and long short-term memory (LSTM) layers. 
This 3D-CLoST model is trained to predict demand more detailed and realistically than the baseline model. 
Based on these prediction models, a simple greedy heuristic assigns relocation workers to pick-up and drop-off zones to perform rebalancing. 
Therefore, the prediction model predicts drop-offs and pick-ups in the next hour for each of the 200 x 200 m zones. 
Based on these predictions and the current number of e-scooters at each zone, the greedy algorithm selects the zones with the highest imbalance between the predicted and actual number of e-scooters. 
These zones are greedily assigned to the nearest relocation worker. This rebalancing is done every hour. 
The rebalancing based on the predictions of the 3D-CLoST model significantly improved the number of possible trips. 
This suggests that even modest rebalancing algorithms, such as the greedy approach, can be significantly improved by precisely predicting future demand. 
However, the simple greedy rebalancing heuristic is not compared to other approaches. 
The greedy heuristic does not adequately capture the complex nature of micromobility sharing systems and may therefore be limited in its applicability to real-life sharing systems.\\

Heuristic-based rebalancing algorithms have demonstrated that lightweight meta-heuristics can lead to near-optimal fleet distributions with significantly reduced computational time compared to mathematical optimization techniques.
Such rebalancing algorithms predominantly focus on operator-based rebalancing under static demand conditions, aiming to find optimal routes for staff vehicles to rebalance the sharing system, often by formulating the problem as a TSP.
In many frameworks, the rebalancing problem is decomposed into separate subtasks, most commonly the independent optimization of each service vehicle's route, so that a single algorithm detects regions of under- or oversupply, proposes fleet relocations, and computes vehicle tours.
This monolithic approach, however, can compromise the overall quality of the solution, as merging imbalance detection, fleet distribution optimization, and route planning often results in a loss of accuracy.
In contrast, concentrating on a single subproblem often yields much more accurate and efficient results.

\section{Reinforcement Learning Approaches}\label{sec:related-work_rl}
Losapio et al. \cite{losapio_smart_2022} present the Multi Agent Deep Reinforcement Learning system called E-Scooter Balancing Deep Q-Network (ESB-DQN). 
This system consists of two agents: pick-up and drop-off agents. These agents suggest an alternative zone for picking up and dropping off e-scooters. 
These suggestions are limited to the adjacent 1-hopping zones. 
Users receive incentives to pick-up or drop-off e-scooters in zones different from those initially planned. 
Losapio et al. divide the operational area for the dockless e-scooter service into 279 zones, each measuring 200 x 200 meters. 
They implement both agents as Deep Q-Networks (DQNs), utilizing an $\epsilon$-greedy strategy combined with experience replay. 
The DQNs follow the Rainbow DQN architecture. 
The action space contains the eight neighboring zones and a no-operation action. 
The state defines three vectors of size 1 × n, where n is the number of zones. 
The first vector represents the number of available scooters, the second represents the expected deficit or surplus based on demand forecasting, and the third is a binary column flagging zones involved in the current trip. 
Whenever a trip request occurs, the pick-up and drop-off agents recommend a different adjacent zone. 
After that, the simulator processes the trip requests and pick-up/drop-off recommendations and transitions to the next state.

The ESB-DQN is evaluated in a simulation environment based on the ODySSEUS simulator introduced by Ciociola et al. \cite{ciociola_e-scooter_2020}, which will be discussed in more detail in \autoref{sec:related-work_simulator}. 
The work accounts for user-based rebalancing, operator-based rebalancing, and battery swaps. 
The system uses a priority queue to handle both rebalancing and battery swapping.
It assigns each crew of workers a schedule modeled and optimized by a TSP.
Rebalancing and battery swapping operations take place within a specified time window, typically standard work hours (e.g., 9:00 am to 5:00 pm).
The description does not specify which solver tackles the TSP.
\\

Cederle et al. \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025} implemented an RL approach to improve the fairness of micromobility sharing services. 
They address the problem of earlier rebalancing algorithms, primarily focusing on efficiency and profit maximization instead of a fair, distributed fleet.
Such rebalancing algorithms tend to prioritize rebalancing to wealthier areas while excluding poorer regions, due to the differing demand rates in those areas.
To counteract this bias, Cederle et al. introduced a fairness metric and integrated it, alongside traditional efficiency measures, into Q-learning agents that perform rebalancing tasks. 
Instead of employing a classic multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) approach, they utilize one agent for each zone. 
Each agent undergoes independent training, which breaks the MARL problem into multiple single-agent subproblems. 
They formulated the issue first as a multi-agent MDP and factored it into single-agent subproblems. 
The fairness metric employed is the Gini index, which is combined with a weighting parameter to balance the optimization of fairness and efficiency. 
The reward function consists of this fairness metric, rebalancing cost, penalties for unmet demand, and penalties for overcrowding. 
Each agent chooses between thirteen different actions, representing a need or an oversupply of vehicles in steps of size five, ranging from -30 to 30. 
Each agent operates independently, and the system assumes having a depot of vehicles to overcome a mismatch of the exact number of vehicles allocated to all zones.
They factorize the MDP into multiple subproblems using a MARL approach, which enables them to adapt it to other cities or services with significantly less effort than alternative methods.
By combining fairness and efficiency metrics, they cut inequality by over 80\% while accepting a 30\% rise in costs.
However, they can adjust the trade-off between inequality reduction and cost increase by modifying a weighting parameter.

Cederle et al. introduce the first operational RL framework that integrates fairness metrics to balance shared micromobility fleets.
Their method provides a flexible approach to embedding fairness.
Still, the framework remains quite simple: it relies on a basic demand model, performs static rebalancing only twice a day, and applies only a single fairness metric.
This simplicity highlights the need for more realistic, and dynamic frameworks that incorporate fairness measures in ever-changing environments.
\\

Pan et al. \cite{pan_deep_2019} introduced the Hierarchical Reinforcement Pricing (HRP) algorithm, a hierarchical RL method that addresses user-based rebalancing in large-scale micromobility sharing services.
They partitioned the service area into regions and turned each region into a subproblem, recognizing that single-agent and multi-agent RL struggle with the complexity of large-scale rebalancing.
They then modeled each subproblem as an MDP, defining the state by current bike supply, historical demand, arrivals, the ratio of unmet to met demand in each region, and the remaining incentive budget.
To set region-specific incentives, they designed a continuous action space.
HRP applies a Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) algorithm and decomposes the total Q-value into additive sub-Q-values for each region.
They also incorporated a bias correction model to account for information from neighboring regions and embedded a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) to capture temporal dynamics more effectively.
These design choices increase sampling efficiency and stabilize convergence during training.
Results show a significant improvement compared to the baselines. 
However, the employed approach presents some weaknesses: it relies on predefined assumptions regarding the user willingness functions and historical data distributions, which might not fully capture the real-world variability in user behavior or unexpected shifts in travel patterns, for example, due to special events. 
These weaknesses suggest a research gap in extending such reinforcement learning models to dynamically adapt to real-time data and incorporate more heterogeneous user cost models, as well as in exploring the integration of multi-modal data sources to reflect the complexities of urban mobility.
\\

Xie et al. \cite{xie_censored_2023} used a different approach than the previously discussed papers. 
They proposed a censored semi-bandit model for allocating bikes from a docked bike-sharing system to other zones. 
Therefore, they divided the operating area into 12 different zones. 
The authors formulated a censored semi-bandit problem where each arm corresponds to one of the zones. 
Using a semi-bandit model instead of an MAB in each round, the model can pick multiple arms. 
In each decision round, which is done once a day, the model allocates bikes to each zone. 
The model aims to maximize the cumulated number of successful bike pick-ups.
This model's policy is an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB)- based policy, which adds an exploration bonus to zones where demand is uncertain. 
The authors address the common problem of micromobility service data being censored, which means that the available historical trip or booking data only contains data that has met demand, and is missing data on unmet demand. 
Most of these datasets do not represent unmet or latent demand, which the system could not handle. 
To test and evaluate rebalancing strategies, the unmet demand is of high value because it is what rebalancing algorithms aim to minimize. 
In this research paper, the authors use a nonparametric estimator to estimate the survival function of demand by comparing the frequency of observed pick-ups above a threshold to the number of opportunities to observe such demand. 
This estimate is then used as the exploration bonus for zones with high uncertainty through limited data. 
Xie et al. proposed two extensions: one that adds contextual data, such as weather data affecting user demand, to improve the relocation, and the second that incorporates relocation costs to balance the benefits and costs. 
The proposed UCB policy is compared to benchmark strategies, such as a greedy or naive policy that ignores censored data. 
The results show that the proposed UCB-based policy achieves a higher number of successful pick-ups. 
The model assumes a static demand distribution due to the nature of MABs, which does not reflect the complexity of real-world shared micromobility services. 
Based on the initial exploration, the model is limited in its ability to adjust dynamically in response to changing demand.
\\

Schofield et al. \cite{schofield_handling_2021} developed an incentive-based rebalancing approach using three different RL algorithms. 
These three RL algorithms are Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO), Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), and Actor-Critic using Kronecker-Factored Trust Region (ACKTR). 
The service area is partitioned into zones using a spatial grid, and time is discretized into hourly intervals.
The incentive mechanism is formulated as an MDP, with the state comprising vectors representing the number of bikes per zone, forecasts of future demand and supply, the remaining budget, and unsatisfied demand.
The action is a vector that assigns an incentive value to each zone. 
The service level defines the reward, the fraction of demand satisfied. 
The three RL algorithms are compared to each other, as well as two baselines. The baselines are set as no incentives, representing the lower bound, and maximum incentives without any budget, representing the upper bound. 
All three algorithms show similar final results, with the PPO algorithm converging the fastest. 
When tested with a limited supply of bicycles, the rebalancing algorithms showed the most improvement. 
The authors use a relatively simple approach to simulate the bike-sharing system. 
The system is simulated using a discrete network and hourly time steps. 
Trip requests are processed hourly. 
For successful trip requests, the corresponding bike is marked as unavailable for the next time step and moved to the destination of the trip requests. 
State of charge, trip duration, and similar properties are not modeled in this simulator, resulting in a minimal simulation scenario.
\\

Li et al. \cite {li_dynamic_2018} propose an approach that uses spatiotemporal reinforcement learning (STRL) to optimize fleet distribution in bike-sharing services dynamically. 
The core methodology begins with a two-stage clustering process - called Inter-Independent Inner-Balance (IIIB) - in which geographically close stations with similar demand characteristics are grouped into balanced clusters, each of which is treated independently to reduce complexity. 
The second stage incorporates predictive modeling through two components: an O-model, which uses a similarity-based k-nearest neighbor approach to forecast pick-up demand, and an I-model, which predicts drop-off demand by probabilistically tracking bike transitions across regions. 
A central element of their approach is a deep Q-learning-based framework, in which individual service vehicles, modeled as agents, utilize historical observations and real-time predictive insights to determine optimal repositioning actions.
This implicitly coordinates their actions by considering each other's states and planned moves. 
Evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach, which significantly reduces unmet demand compared to heuristic repositioning strategies. 
However, the proposed model also reveals limitations. 
While the authors implement spatio-temporal pruning heuristics to reduce computational complexity, scalability remains constrained by the exponential growth of state-action spaces as system size increases. 
Sensitivity to prediction accuracy for demand forecasts and potential inefficiencies resulting from asynchronous decision-making among multiple agents can impact the effectiveness of the framework.
\\

The recent research discussed both single-agent and multi-agent solutions for rebalancing of shared micromobility fleets.
Single-agent approaches either use only one agent for the entire system or, more commonly, decompose the rebalancing problem into multiple subproblems, which are solved independently by separate agents.
Similar to Loaspio et al. \cite{losapio_smart_2022}, where one agent focused on pick-up and one agent focused on drop-off locations, or as done by Pan et al. \cite{pan_deep_2019}, which uses one agent for each geographic zone.
This factorization of the rebalancing problem reduces computational complexity and simplifies the overall RL framework.
However, these simplifications come at the cost of missing interdependencies between geographical zones or interacting behaviour.
Multi-agent approaches, on the other hand, model multiple decision-makers that learn concurrently, being aware of each other and taking the actions of other agents into account.
This collaborative approach can capture more realistic system dynamics of shared micromobility services, such as shared resources, coordinated incentives, or a shared strategy for rebalancing.
One approach described by Li et al. \cite{li_dynamic_2018} built a MARL framework where each agent corresponded to one of the service vehicles, which relocate the sharing vehicles. 
Their approach utilized a shared state representation, which also informed the agents about each other's intended actions to learn to balance the whole fleet collaboratively.
While MARL frameworks better reflect real-world scenarios, they also require more computational resources and tend to have issues with stability in convergence and scalability.
Ultimately, the decision between single-agent and multi-agent approaches for rebalancing shared micromobility fleets remains a trade-off between their advantages and disadvantages, with neither approach proving superior to the other.

In summary, existing literature on reinforcement learning (RL)-based rebalancing algorithms predominantly emphasizes user-based strategies, seeking optimal incentives for users to pick-up and drop-off vehicles at alternative locations.
Conversely, research explicitly targeting operator-based rebalancing remains limited, with even fewer studies investigating hybrid approaches combining both user and operator rebalancing.
Value-based algorithms, such as Deep Q-Networks (DQN) and Q-learning, and policy-gradient methods, including Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO), and Actor-Critic using Kronecker-Factored Trust Region (ACKTR), form the most frequently applied methodologies.
Recent studies have explored hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) and multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) frameworks to decompose complex rebalancing tasks into manageable subtasks addressed by multiple agents.
Such hierarchical and multi-agent approaches have demonstrated enhanced scalability and effectiveness in managing large-scale shared mobility services  \cite{pan_deep_2019, li_dynamic_2018}.

RL-based methods typically outperform mathematical optimization and heuristic-based strategies
However, comparative evaluations remain limited, restricted to only a few published studies \cite{li_dynamic_2018, pan_deep_2019, liang_dual_2024}.
Furthermore, fairness considerations in fleet distribution have been almost never addressed, with Cederle et al. \cite{cederle_fairness-oriented_2025} presenting the only framework that incorporates fairness metrics to improve fleet distribution.
Comprehensive analyses balancing operational costs against fairness considerations are notably absent in current research
Additionally, existing approaches predominantly utilize historical demand data or coarse-grained forecasts, resulting in limited responsiveness to real-time demand fluctuations.
Consequently, future research should prioritize the development of dynamic demand forecasting methods, thereby enhancing the adaptability and responsiveness of rebalancing algorithms to shifting demand conditions.
\\

\section{Simulation Environments}\label{sec:related-work_simulator}
Developing and tuning rebalancing frameworks is challenging due to the complex dynamics of urban demand and the high costs associated with operational trials.
High-fidelity simulation environments can be utilized to evaluate rebalancing methods prior to their deployment in the real world.
Simulation enables safe and repeatable testing of rebalancing frameworks under various scenarios.
This section introduces the most commonly used simulation frameworks for evaluating rebalancing frameworks.

\subsubsection{SUMO}
The Simulation of Urban Mobility (SUMO) is an open-source, microscopic traffic simulation platform widely used in mobility research \cite{alvarez_lopez_simulation_2025}.
SUMO models individual vehicle movements with high granularity, using car-following and lane-changing models to reproduce realistic traffic dynamics.
SUMO can be vastly extended due to its modularity and open-source nature.
An external control API (TraCI) enables users to observe and update the current state via Python scripts, which opens up numerous opportunities, such as incorporating rebalancing algorithms.
Overall, SUMO provides a high-fidelity representation of urban mobility, making it well-suited for evaluating how rebalancing frameworks perform under realistic conditions.
SUMO does not explicitly offer agent-based modeling of users or vehicles, but could be extended via TraCI.

SUMO has been utilized primarily in studies for traffic simulation.
\\

\subsubsection{ODySSEUS}
ODySSEUS is a specialized simulation environment focused on the operations of shared micromobility systems \cite{smartdatapolito_smartdatapolitoodysseus_2022}.
ODySSEUS focuses on modeling the fleet and service dynamics of shared micromobility systems, including demand generation and forecasting, vehicle utilization, battery charging, and rebalancing operations.
While ODySSEUS cannot simulate per-vehicle traffic interactions at the level of SUMO's road network, it can integrate simplified mobility models to approximate travel delays.
Its strength lies in modeling the specific operational complexities of micro-mobility sharing systems, such as peak demand periods, staff schedules, and depot logistics for battery swapping and spare vehicles.

Losapio et al. \cite{losapio_smart_2022} employed a simulation environment based on ODySSEUS to evaluate their ESB-DQN rebalancing framework. 
They discretized the operational area using a grid and implemented individual algorithms for both battery swapping and rebalancing, rather than utilizing the built-in queue systems.
This simulator is then wrapped inside a gym environment for training and evaluating the ESB-DQN framework.
However, utilizing ODySSEUS for training resulted in a very long training process, which is often unfeasible and costly.
\\

\subsubsection{MATSim}
MATSim (Multi-Agent Transport Simulation) is an open-source, activity-based, multi-agent simulator that models travel demand and network loading at the city scale.
It represents each traveler as an agent with a daily plan of activities, and simulates their mode choice and routes in an iterative way.
Its level of detail is coarser than SUMO's.
Traffic flow is simulated using queue models, rather than through explicit vehicle-tracking.
While MATSim does not natively model operational interventions, such as rebalancing, its flexible architecture allows for custom modules to do so.
MATSim is Java-based, which makes it harder to integrate RL or similar algorithms, which are most commonly written in Python.

MATSim has been used in several studies for evaluating rebalancing algorithms \cite{chouaki_review_2025}.
\\

\subsubsection{Summary}
Several platforms have been used in recent research for simulating shared micromobility fleets, enabling the testing and evaluation of various rebalancing strategies.
SUMO is a microscopic traffic simulator that enables high-fidelity analysis of detailed vehicle interactions, energy consumption (especially relevant for electric fleets considering factors like elevation), and the localized traffic impacts of rebalancing operations.
However, implementing specific service logic and rebalancing strategies typically requires custom development via its TraCI interface.
ODySSEUS provides a specialized environment explicitly focused on the operational dynamics of shared fleets, leveraging real-world data to model and optimize fleet performance metrics, such as vehicle availability and rebalancing efficiency.
This makes it particularly suitable for data-driven analysis from a service provider's perspective.
Nevertheless, ODySSEUS simulates at a higher abstraction level than SUMO, which affects the realism of simulations performed using ODySSEUS.
In contrast, MATSim employs a large-scale, agent-based, mesoscopic approach centered on modeling traveler behavior and activity planning.
The program's strength lies in simulating how changes in service attributes affect user mode choice, overall travel patterns, demand satisfaction, and the system's equilibrium state.
This makes it ideal for strategic analysis and exploring user responses to incentives.
The implementation of custom rebalancing frameworks is more complex because MATSim is Java-based, whereas machine learning and similar algorithms are predominantly written in Python.


\section{Summary and Research Gaps}
Almost all examined research papers propose a system to optimize user-based or operator-based rebalancing in isolation, without a proper framework for both rebalancing approaches.
Heuristic and mathematical optimization approaches use very simplified problem definitions. RL approaches tend to use more complex representations of the sharing service and, therefore, can account for more dynamic environments than the other two.
All research papers propose a simulation environment to evaluate their rebalancing algorithms.
These simulation environments replicate the complexity of micromobility sharing services in a simplified manner, varying in the degree of simplification.
Another key component of most research work is a demand model.
All algorithms highly depend on user demand to train and test them.
Therefore, the accuracy of these demand models can vastly affect the effectiveness of the rebalancing algorithms.
Most demand models are based on historical demand, often involving machine learning techniques to extract demand patterns to generate new user demand.
This reliance on historical demand has the problem of censored demand, as historical demand can only represent the demand that the sharing service can actually satisfy.
Unmet demand is almost always not represented in historical demand data. 
This missing representation poses a significant problem when optimizing rebalancing algorithms, as the sharing service aims to minimize unmet demand through these algorithms.
Therefore, accurately representing satisfied and unmet demand is critical for optimizing rebalancing algorithms.
